**Context:** This comprehensive reference explains the disease mechanisms of cardiovascular conditions commonly addressed with herbal medicine. Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of death globally, making understanding of these pathophysiological processes essential for effective herbal interventions. **Purpose:** To provide detailed pathophysiological understanding of:
- Hypertension and blood pressure regulation
- Atherosclerosis and plaque formation
- Coronary artery disease and myocardial infarction
- Heart failure (systolic and diastolic)
- Arrhythmias and conduction abnormalities
- Dyslipidemia and lipid metabolism disorders
- Peripheral arterial disease
- Endothelial dysfunction as common pathway

# Blood Pressure Disorders {#bp-disorders}

## Hypertension {#hypertension}

### Definition and Classification

**Hypertension** is sustained elevation of blood pressure. It is the **most common cardiovascular disease** and major risk factor for stroke, myocardial infarction, heart failure, chronic kidney disease, and premature death.

**2017 ACC/AHA Blood Pressure Classification:**

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|----------|-----------------|---|------------------|
| **Normal** | <120 | AND | <80 |
| **Elevated** | 120-129 | AND | <80 |
| **Stage 1 Hypertension** | 130-139 | OR | 80-89 |
| **Stage 2 Hypertension** | ≥140 | OR | ≥90 |
| **Hypertensive Crisis** | >180 | AND/OR | >120 |

**Epidemiology:**
- Affects ~45% of US adults (116 million Americans)
- Prevalence increases with age (>65% in those >60 years)
- Higher in Black Americans than other ethnic groups
- **"Silent killer"**: Often asymptomatic until complications occur

### Normal Blood Pressure Regulation

**Blood Pressure Formula:**
- **BP = Cardiac Output (CO) × Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR)**
- CO = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume
- SVR = determined by arteriolar diameter (vasoconstriction/vasodilation)

**Regulatory Systems:**

#### 1. Baroreceptor Reflex (Short-Term Regulation)

**Mechanism:**
- **Baroreceptors**: Stretch receptors in carotid sinus and aortic arch
- Detect changes in blood pressure
- **↑ BP** → baroreceptors fire more frequently → medulla oblongata → ↑ parasympathetic, ↓ sympathetic → ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, vasodilation → ↓ BP
- **↓ BP** → baroreceptors fire less → ↑ sympathetic, ↓ parasympathetic → ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, vasoconstriction → ↑ BP
- **Response time**: Seconds to minutes
- **Limitation**: Resets to new baseline after 1-2 days (not effective for long-term BP control)

#### 2. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) (Long-Term Regulation)

**Trigger:** ↓ BP, ↓ renal perfusion, ↑ sympathetic activity

**Cascade:**

1. **Kidney releases renin** (from juxtaglomerular cells)
2. **Renin converts angiotensinogen → angiotensin I** (produced by liver)
3. **ACE converts angiotensin I → angiotensin II** (primarily in lungs)
4. **Angiotensin II actions:**
   - **Direct vasoconstriction** (most potent vasoconstrictor in body) → ↑ SVR → ↑ BP (immediate)
   - **Stimulates aldosterone release** from adrenal cortex → Na⁺ and H₂O retention → ↑ blood volume → ↑ BP (hours to days)
   - **Stimulates ADH (vasopressin) release** from posterior pituitary → H₂O retention → ↑ blood volume
   - **Stimulates thirst** → ↑ fluid intake
   - **Promotes sympathetic activity**
   - **Cardiac and vascular remodeling** (hypertrophy, fibrosis) - contributes to end-organ damage

**Clinical Importance:**
- **ACE inhibitors** (-pril drugs): Block conversion of angiotensin I → II
- **ARBs** (angiotensin receptor blockers, -sartan drugs): Block angiotensin II receptors
- First-line antihypertensive drugs

#### 3. Natriuretic Peptides

**ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide) and BNP (Brain Natriuretic Peptide):**
- Released by atria (ANP) and ventricles (BNP) in response to stretch (volume overload)
- **Actions**:
  - ↑ Renal Na⁺ and H₂O excretion (natriuresis, diuresis)
  - Vasodilation
  - Inhibit renin and aldosterone secretion
  - **Net effect**: ↓ Blood volume, ↓ SVR → ↓ BP
- **Counterregulatory** to RAAS

#### 4. Sympathetic Nervous System

**Activation:**
- ↑ Heart rate and contractility → ↑ cardiac output
- Vasoconstriction → ↑ SVR
- Stimulates renin release
- **Chronic activation** contributes to hypertension

#### 5. Endothelial Function

**Nitric Oxide (NO):**
- Produced by vascular endothelium (endothelial nitric oxide synthase - eNOS)
- **Actions**:
  - Potent vasodilator
  - Antiplatelet (prevents aggregation)
  - Anti-inflammatory
  - Inhibits vascular smooth muscle proliferation
- **Endothelial dysfunction** → ↓ NO production → vasoconstriction → hypertension

**Endothelin-1:**
- Produced by endothelium
- Potent vasoconstrictor
- ↑ In hypertension, heart failure, atherosclerosis

### Pathophysiology of Hypertension

#### Essential (Primary) Hypertension (90-95% of cases)

**Definition:** Hypertension with no identifiable cause; multifactorial

**Contributing Mechanisms:**

**1. Increased Sympathetic Nervous System Activity:**
- Chronic stress → sustained sympathetic activation
- ↑ Heart rate, ↑ contractility, vasoconstriction, ↑ renin release
- **Pathogenic sequence**: Sustained ↑ BP → baroreceptor reset → new "normal" elevated BP

**2. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System Dysregulation:**
- Inappropriately elevated activity
- ↑ Angiotensin II → chronic vasoconstriction, Na⁺ retention, vascular remodeling
- ↑ Aldosterone → Na⁺ and H₂O retention → ↑ blood volume

**3. Endothelial Dysfunction:**
- ↓ Nitric oxide production or bioavailability
- ↑ Endothelin-1
- ↑ Reactive oxygen species (ROS) inactivate NO
- **Result**: Impaired vasodilation, vasoconstriction predominates

**4. Increased Vascular Stiffness:**
- Arteriosclerosis (age-related stiffening of arteries)
- ↓ Arterial compliance → ↑ systolic BP
- **Isolated systolic hypertension** in elderly (systolic ≥140, diastolic <90)

**5. Renal Dysfunction:**
- Impaired Na⁺ excretion ("pressure-natriuresis" curve shifted)
- Requires higher BP to excrete same amount of Na⁺
- Leads to salt-sensitive hypertension

**6. Insulin Resistance:**
- Hyperinsulinemia → ↑ sympathetic activity, ↑ Na⁺ retention, ↑ vascular smooth muscle proliferation
- Common in metabolic syndrome, obesity, type 2 diabetes

**7. Inflammation and Oxidative Stress:**
- Chronic low-grade inflammation
- ↑ Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, CRP)
- ↑ Oxidative stress → endothelial dysfunction
- Contributes to vascular remodeling

**8. Genetic Factors:**
- Heritability ~30-50%
- Multiple genes involved (no single "hypertension gene")
- Genes affecting: RAAS, sodium transport, sympathetic activity, vascular tone

**Risk Factors for Essential Hypertension:**

| Non-Modifiable | Modifiable |
|----------------|------------|
| Age (↑ with age) | Obesity (BMI ≥30) |
| Family history | High sodium intake (>2300 mg/day) |
| Black race/ethnicity | Physical inactivity |
| Male sex (until menopause; then females ↑) | Excessive alcohol (>2 drinks/day men, >1 women) |
| | Smoking |
| | Chronic stress |
| | Poor diet (low K⁺, Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺) |
| | Insulin resistance/diabetes |

#### Secondary Hypertension (5-10% of cases)

**Definition:** Hypertension with identifiable underlying cause

**Causes:**

**1. Renal Causes (Most Common Secondary Cause):**
- **Chronic kidney disease**: ↓ GFR → ↓ Na⁺ excretion, ↑ volume overload
- **Renovascular disease**: Renal artery stenosis → ↓ renal perfusion → ↑ renin → hypertension
  - Atherosclerotic (elderly)
  - Fibromuscular dysplasia (young women)
- **Polycystic kidney disease**
- **Glomerulonephritis**

**2. Endocrine Causes:**
- **Primary aldosteronism (Conn syndrome)**:
  - Adrenal adenoma or hyperplasia
  - ↑ Aldosterone → Na⁺ retention, K⁺ loss
  - Hypertension + hypokalemia
  - Screening: Plasma aldosterone:renin ratio (ARR)
- **Pheochromocytoma**:
  - Adrenal tumor secreting catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine)
  - **Episodic hypertension** with headache, palpitations, sweating
  - 24-hour urine metanephrines
- **Cushing syndrome**:
  - Excess cortisol (endogenous or exogenous)
  - Cortisol has mineralocorticoid activity
- **Hyperthyroidism**:
  - ↑ Cardiac output → ↑ systolic BP
- **Hypothyroidism**:
  - ↑ Diastolic BP (↑ SVR)
- **Hyperparathyroidism**:
  - ↑ Calcium → ↑ vascular smooth muscle contractility

**3. Coarctation of Aorta:**
- Congenital narrowing of descending aorta
- ↑ BP in upper extremities, ↓ BP in lower extremities
- **Classic finding**: BP differential between arms and legs

**4. Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA):**
- Intermittent hypoxia → sympathetic activation, endothelial dysfunction, inflammation
- Strong association with resistant hypertension

**5. Medications and Substances:**
- **NSAIDs**: Na⁺ retention, inhibit prostaglandin-mediated vasodilation
- **Oral contraceptives**: Estrogen-related
- **Corticosteroids**: Mineralocorticoid effects
- **Decongestants** (pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine): Sympathomimetic
- **Stimulants**: Cocaine, amphetamines
- **Licorice** (glycyrrhizin): Mineralocorticoid-like effects
- **Erythropoietin**
- **Cyclosporine, tacrolimus**: Immunosuppressants

**Clues to Secondary Hypertension:**
- Age <30 or >55 with new-onset hypertension
- Severe or resistant hypertension
- Hypertension with hypokalemia (think aldosteronism)
- Abrupt onset or worsening
- Abdominal bruit (renovascular)
- BP discrepancy between limbs (coarctation)

### Clinical Presentation

**Most Patients: ASYMPTOMATIC**
- Hypertension discovered incidentally
- **"Silent killer"** - no symptoms until complications

**Symptoms (if present, usually indicate severe or rapidly rising BP):**
- Headache (occipital, morning)
- Dizziness
- Blurred vision
- Dyspnea (if heart failure developing)
- Chest pain (if coronary disease)
- Nosebleeds (epistaxis)

**Physical Exam Findings:**
- Elevated BP on multiple readings (confirm on ≥2 occasions)
- Fundoscopic exam: Hypertensive retinopathy (arterial narrowing, AV nicking, hemorrhages, exudates, papilledema)
- Cardiac exam: Left ventricular heave (LVH), S4 gallop
- Vascular: Diminished or delayed femoral pulses (coarctation), bruits (renovascular)

### Target Organ Damage (Complications)

**Chronic hypertension damages:**

**1. Heart:**
- **Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)**:
  - Heart muscle thickens to overcome increased afterload
  - Eventually → diastolic dysfunction → systolic dysfunction → heart failure
  - ↑ Risk of arrhythmias, sudden cardiac death
- **Coronary Artery Disease**: Accelerates atherosclerosis
- **Myocardial Infarction**
- **Congestive Heart Failure**

**2. Brain:**
- **Stroke**: #1 complication of hypertension
  - Ischemic stroke (atherosclerosis, thrombosis)
  - Hemorrhagic stroke (ruptured aneurysm or vessel)
- **Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA)**
- **Vascular dementia**
- **Hypertensive encephalopathy**: Acute brain dysfunction from severe BP elevation

**3. Kidneys:**
- **Hypertensive Nephropathy**:
  - Chronic kidney damage from prolonged hypertension
  - Glomerulosclerosis (scarring of glomeruli)
  - Progressive ↓ GFR → chronic kidney disease
  - May progress to end-stage renal disease (ESRD)
- Creates vicious cycle: Hypertension → kidney damage → worsened hypertension

**4. Eyes:**
- **Hypertensive Retinopathy**:
  - Grade 1-2: Mild arterial narrowing
  - Grade 3: Hemorrhages, exudates (cotton-wool spots)
  - Grade 4: Papilledema (optic disc swelling) - hypertensive emergency
- Vision loss

**5. Vasculature:**
- **Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)**
- **Aortic Dissection**: Life-threatening tear in aortic wall
- **Aortic Aneurysm**: Dilation and potential rupture

### Diagnosis

**BP Measurement Technique (Critical):**
- Patient seated, back supported, feet flat, arm at heart level
- Appropriate cuff size (bladder covers 80% of arm circumference)
- Rest 5 minutes before measurement
- Average ≥2 readings on ≥2 separate occasions
- Measure both arms (use higher reading)

**Ambulatory BP Monitoring (ABPM) or Home BP Monitoring:**
- **Gold standard** for confirming diagnosis
- Rules out "white coat hypertension" (elevated only in clinic)
- Identifies "masked hypertension" (normal in clinic, elevated at home)

**Initial Evaluation:**

**Laboratory Tests:**
- **Serum chemistry**: Creatinine, eGFR (kidney function), electrolytes (K⁺)
- **Fasting glucose** or HbA1c: Diabetes screening
- **Lipid panel**: Cardiovascular risk assessment
- **Urinalysis**: Proteinuria, hematuria (kidney damage)
- **Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio**: Early kidney damage
- **TSH**: Rule out thyroid disease
- **CBC**: Baseline

**Electrocardiogram (ECG):**
- Assess for left ventricular hypertrophy, previous MI, arrhythmias

**Echocardiogram (if indicated):**
- Assess LV structure and function if LVH suspected

**Secondary Hypertension Workup (if indicated):**
- Renal ultrasound with Doppler (renovascular disease)
- Plasma aldosterone:renin ratio (primary aldosteronism)
- 24-hour urine metanephrines (pheochromocytoma)
- Sleep study (obstructive sleep apnea)

### Treatment

**Goal BP Targets:**
- **General population**: <130/80 mmHg
- **High-risk patients** (diabetes, CKD, CAD): <130/80 mmHg
- **Elderly (≥65)**: Individualized; systolic <130 if tolerated

#### Lifestyle Modifications (First-Line for All Patients)

**DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):**
- ↑ Fruits, vegetables (8-10 servings/day)
- ↑ Whole grains
- ↑ Low-fat dairy
- ↓ Saturated fat, total fat
- ↓ Red meat
- ↑ Nuts, beans, seeds
- **Efficacy**: ↓ Systolic BP by 11 mmHg

**Sodium Restriction:**
- **Goal**: <2300 mg/day (ideal <1500 mg/day)
- **Efficacy**: ↓ Systolic BP by 5-6 mmHg

**Weight Loss:**
- **Goal**: Achieve healthy BMI (18.5-24.9)
- **Efficacy**: ↓ 1 mmHg per 1 kg weight loss

**Physical Activity:**
- **Aerobic exercise**: ≥150 min/week moderate intensity OR ≥75 min/week vigorous
- **Resistance training**: 2-3 days/week
- **Efficacy**: ↓ Systolic BP by 5-8 mmHg

**Limit Alcohol:**
- **Men**: ≤2 drinks/day
- **Women**: ≤1 drink/day
- **Efficacy**: ↓ Systolic BP by 4 mmHg

**Smoking Cessation:**
- Essential for overall cardiovascular risk reduction

**Stress Reduction:**
- Meditation, yoga, mindfulness, biofeedback

#### Pharmacological Treatment

**First-Line Antihypertensive Classes:**

**1. Thiazide Diuretics:**
- Examples: Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), chlorthalidone
- Mechanism: ↓ Na⁺ and H₂O reabsorption → ↓ blood volume
- Side effects: Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia

**2. ACE Inhibitors (-pril):**
- Examples: Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril
- Mechanism: Block angiotensin I → II conversion
- **Benefits**: Renoprotective, cardioprotective
- Side effects: Dry cough (10-20%), hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare)
- **Contraindicated**: Pregnancy (teratogenic)

**3. Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs, -sartan):**
- Examples: Losartan, valsartan, olmesartan
- Mechanism: Block angiotensin II receptors
- **Advantages**: Similar benefits to ACE-I, no cough
- Side effects: Hyperkalemia, hypotension
- **Contraindicated**: Pregnancy

**4. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs):**
- **Dihydropyridines** (-dipine): Amlodipine, nifedipine
  - Mechanism: Vasodilation (peripheral arterioles)
  - Side effects: Peripheral edema, headache, flushing
- **Non-dihydropyridines**: Diltiazem, verapamil
  - Mechanism: ↓ Heart rate, ↓ contractility, vasodilation
  - Side effects: Bradycardia, constipation, heart block

**Combination Therapy:**
- Most patients require ≥2 drugs
- **Synergistic combinations**:
  - ACE-I or ARB + CCB
  - ACE-I or ARB + thiazide diuretic
  - CCB + thiazide diuretic
- **Avoid**: ACE-I + ARB (↑ hyperkalemia, no added benefit)

**Resistant Hypertension:**
- **Definition**: BP not controlled despite ≥3 drugs (including diuretic) at optimal doses
- **Causes**: Non-adherence, secondary hypertension, obstructive sleep apnea, excessive Na⁺ intake
- **Treatment**: Add spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist)

### Comprehensive Herbal Protocol for Hypertension

**Core Cardiovascular Herbs:**

**1. Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.):**
- **Dose**: 160-900 mg standardized extract (oligomeric procyanidins) BID-TID, OR 4-5 mL tincture TID
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Mild ACE inhibition
  - Vasodilation (↑ NO, calcium channel blockade)
  - Positive inotrope (↑ cardiac contractility without ↑ O₂ demand)
  - Antioxidant (flavonoids)
  - Anti-arrhythmic
- **Evidence**: Grade B evidence for mild hypertension and heart failure
- **Onset**: 6-12 weeks for full effect (chronic use herb)
- **Safety**: Excellent; safe with cardiac medications
- **Clinical pearl**: Gold standard cardiovascular tonic; cardioprotective

**2. Hibiscus (Hibiscus sabdariffa):**
- **Dose**: 1-2 tsp dried flowers as tea TID, OR 250-500 mg extract BID
- **Mechanisms**:
  - **Significant ACE inhibition** (comparable to captopril in some studies)
  - Diuretic effect
  - Antioxidant (anthocyanins)
  - ↓ LDL oxidation
- **Evidence**: **Grade A** - Multiple RCTs show efficacy
  - **Study** (McKay et al., 2010): ↓ Systolic BP by 7.5 mmHg, ↓ diastolic by 3.5 mmHg (6 weeks)
- **Safety**: Excellent; safe long-term
- **Clinical pearl**: Evidence-based herb for stage 1 hypertension

**3. Olive Leaf (Olea europaea):**
- **Dose**: 500-1000 mg standardized extract (oleuropein) daily
- **Mechanisms**:
  - ACE inhibition
  - Vasodilation (↑ NO)
  - Antioxidant
  - Mild diuretic
- **Evidence**: RCTs show comparable efficacy to captopril for stage 1 hypertension
- **Safety**: Well-tolerated

**4. Garlic (Allium sativum):**
- **Dose**: 600-1200 mg aged garlic extract daily, OR 1-2 raw cloves
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Vasodilation (H₂S production, ↑ NO)
  - Mild ACE inhibition
  - Antioxidant
  - Antiplatelet
  - Lipid-lowering
- **Evidence**: Meta-analysis shows ↓ systolic BP by 8.3 mmHg, ↓ diastolic by 5.5 mmHg
- **Clinical pearl**: Addresses multiple cardiovascular risk factors

**5. Celery Seed (Apium graveolens):**
- **Dose**: 500-1000 mg extract BID, OR 2-4 mL tincture TID
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Vasodilation (3-n-butylphthalide)
  - Diuretic
  - Calcium channel blockade
- **Traditional use**: Specific for hypertension in Traditional Chinese Medicine
- **Safety**: Avoid in pregnancy (uterine stimulant), kidney disease

**Adaptogenic Support (if stress-related hypertension):**

**6. Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera):**

- **Mechanisms**:
  - HPA axis modulation → ↓ cortisol

- **Indication**: Hypertension with anxiety, chronic stress, insomnia
- **Evidence**: ↓ Cortisol, ↓ perceived stress, improves cardiovascular parameters

**7. Holy Basil (Ocimum sanctum):**

**9. Linden (Tilia spp.):**

**Nutritional Support:**

**11. Magnesium:**
- **Dose**: 300-600 mg daily (glycinate, taurate, or malate)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Natural calcium channel blocker

**12. Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10):**

**13. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA):**
- **Dose**: 2-4g daily (combined EPA+DHA)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Anti-inflammatory

**14. Potassium:**
- **Dietary sources**: Bananas, avocados, sweet potatoes, spinach, beans

- **Mechanism**: ↑ Na⁺ excretion, vasodilation
- **Caution**: Avoid supplementation if on ACE-I, ARB, or spironolactone (risk of hyperkalemia)

### Clinical Evidence for Herbal Antihypertensives

**Hibiscus (Hibiscus sabdariffa):**
- **Meta-analysis** (Serban et al., 2015): Hibiscus consumption ↓ systolic BP by 7.58 mmHg and diastolic by 3.53 mmHg
- **RCT** (McKay et al., 2010): 65 adults with prehypertension/mild hypertension; hibiscus tea (3 cups daily) ↓ systolic BP by 7.2 mmHg vs placebo
- **Grade A evidence**

**Hawthorn (Crataegus):**
- **Cochrane Review** (Pittler et al., 2008): Trend toward BP reduction in patients with diabetes taking hawthorn extract
- Long history of traditional use; supportive evidence
- **Grade B evidence**

**Garlic (Allium sativum):**
- **Meta-analysis** (Ried et al., 2016): Garlic supplements ↓ systolic BP by 8.3 mmHg and diastolic by 5.5 mmHg in hypertensive patients
- **Dose-dependent**: ≥600 mg/day for ≥8 weeks

**Olive Leaf (Olea europaea):**
- **RCT** (Susalit et al., 2011): Olive leaf extract (500mg BID) comparable efficacy to captopril 25mg BID for stage 1 hypertension

**CoQ10:**
- **Meta-analysis** (Rosenfeldt et al., 2007): CoQ10 supplementation ↓ systolic BP by 11 mmHg and diastolic by 7 mmHg
- Variable study quality; larger, high-quality trials needed

5. **Sleep optimization**: 7-9 hours, address sleep apnea if present
6. **Limit alcohol**: ≤2 drinks/day (men), ≤1 drink/day (women)
7. **Smoking cessation**

**Monitoring:**
- **Home BP monitoring**: Daily (morning and evening) for first 2 weeks, then weekly
- **Target**: <130/80 mmHg
- **Follow-up**: Reassess in 4-8 weeks
- **If no improvement** after 12 weeks of comprehensive lifestyle + herbs → consider adding or intensifying pharmacotherapy

### Hypertensive Emergency {#hypertensive-emergency}

**Definition:** BP >180/120 mmHg **with acute end-organ damage**

**Manifestations:**
- **Hypertensive encephalopathy**: Confusion, seizures, coma
- **Acute stroke**: Ischemic or hemorrhagic
- **Acute MI**: Chest pain, ECG changes, troponin elevation
- **Acute pulmonary edema**: Dyspnea, crackles, hypoxemia
- **Acute kidney injury**: ↑ Creatinine, oliguria
- **Aortic dissection**: Tearing chest/back pain
- **Eclampsia**: Pregnancy-related hypertensive emergency

**Management:**
- **Medical emergency**: Immediate hospitalization, IV antihypertensives
- **Goal**: ↓ BP by 25% in first hour, then gradually to 160/100 over next 2-6 hours
- **Avoid rapid ↓**: Risk of cerebral/coronary hypoperfusion
- **NOT an indication for herbal therapy** - requires immediate medical care

**Clinical Note:** Hypertension is **most common modifiable cardiovascular risk factor**. Stage 1 hypertension may respond to lifestyle + herbs alone. Stage 2 or presence of cardiovascular disease/diabetes usually requires pharmacotherapy. Herbs can be **adjunctive** to reduce medication burden or enhance BP control. **Never discontinue prescribed antihypertensives** without medical supervision. Monitor for additive hypotensive effects when combining herbs with medications. Emphasize lifestyle modifications - most impactful intervention.

## Atherosclerosis {#atherosclerosis}

### Definition

**Atherosclerosis** is a chronic inflammatory disease of large and medium-sized arteries characterized by the formation of atherosclerotic plaques (atheromas) in the arterial wall. It is the underlying pathology for most cardiovascular diseases including coronary artery disease, stroke, and peripheral arterial disease.

### Normal Arterial Structure

**Three Layers:**

**1. Tunica Intima (Innermost):**
- Single layer of **endothelial cells** on basement membrane
- **Endothelium** functions:
  - Barrier between blood and vessel wall
  - Produces **nitric oxide (NO)** → vasodilation, antiplatelet, anti-inflammatory
  - Produces **prostacyclin (PGI₂)** → vasodilation, antiplatelet
  - Regulates vascular tone, permeability, inflammation, thrombosis
- **Healthy endothelium** is anti-inflammatory, antithrombotic

**2. Tunica Media (Middle):**
- Smooth muscle cells
- Elastic fibers
- Regulates vessel diameter (vasoconstriction/vasodilation)

**3. Tunica Adventitia (Outer):**
- Connective tissue
- Vasa vasorum (blood vessels supplying vessel wall)
- Nerve fibers

### Pathophysiology of Atherosclerosis

**Five-Stage Process:**

#### Stage 1: Endothelial Injury and Dysfunction

**Initiating Event:** Damage to endothelial cells

**Causes of Endothelial Injury:**
- **Mechanical stress**:
  - **Hypertension**: Shear stress damages endothelium
  - Turbulent blood flow (at arterial bifurcations, branches)
- **Metabolic factors**:
  - **Hyperlipidemia**: Especially elevated LDL cholesterol, oxidized LDL
  - **Hyperglycemia**: Advanced glycation end products (AGEs) in diabetes
  - **Hyperhomocysteinemia**
- **Inflammatory factors**:
  - Chronic inflammation (elevated CRP, IL-6, TNF-α)
  - Autoimmune conditions
- **Infectious agents** (controversial):
  - Chlamydia pneumoniae, cytomegalovirus, H. pylori
  - May contribute to inflammatory milieu
- **Toxins**:
  - **Smoking**: Most potent modifiable risk factor
  - Oxidative stress from cigarette smoke
  - Carbon monoxide, free radicals
- **Other**:
  - Insulin resistance
  - Obesity (adipokines promote inflammation)

**Consequences of Endothelial Dysfunction:**
- **↓ Nitric oxide (NO) production**:
  - Impaired vasodilation
  - Loss of antiplatelet, anti-inflammatory effects
- **↑ Endothelial permeability**:
  - Allows LDL penetration into arterial wall
- **↑ Expression of adhesion molecules**:
  - VCAM-1 (vascular cell adhesion molecule-1)
  - ICAM-1 (intercellular adhesion molecule-1)
  - Recruit immune cells (monocytes, T-lymphocytes)
- **Pro-thrombotic state**:
  - ↑ Tissue factor, von Willebrand factor
  - ↓ Anticoagulant factors
- **Pro-inflammatory state**:
  - ↑ Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)

#### Stage 2: Lipid Accumulation and Oxidation

**LDL Infiltration:**
- Damaged endothelium → ↑ permeability
- **LDL cholesterol** particles cross into subendothelial space (tunica intima)
- Normal LDL is not inherently atherogenic

**LDL Oxidation (Critical Step):**
- LDL trapped in intima becomes **oxidized** (oxLDL)
- Oxidation by:
  - Reactive oxygen species (ROS)
  - Enzymes (myeloperoxidase, lipoxygenases)
- **Oxidized LDL (oxLDL)**:
  - Highly inflammatory
  - Toxic to endothelial cells
  - Chemoattractant for monocytes
  - Cannot be recognized by normal LDL receptors
  - Recognized by scavenger receptors on macrophages

#### Stage 3: Inflammatory Cell Recruitment and Foam Cell Formation

**Monocyte Recruitment:**
- Adhesion molecules (VCAM-1, ICAM-1) on damaged endothelium bind monocytes
- Monocytes adhere, roll along endothelium
- **MCP-1 (monocyte chemoattractant protein-1)** attracts monocytes
- Monocytes migrate into intima

**Differentiation:**
- Monocytes → **Macrophages** (stimulated by M-CSF)

**Foam Cell Formation:**
- Macrophages express **scavenger receptors** (CD36, SR-A)
- Scavenger receptors bind and internalize oxidized LDL
- **Unregulated uptake**: No negative feedback (unlike LDL receptor)
- Macrophages become engorged with lipid droplets
- **"Foam cells"**: Lipid-laden macrophages (foamy appearance on histology)

**T-Lymphocyte Recruitment:**
- T-cells (especially CD4+) migrate into intima
- Release pro-inflammatory cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-1)
- Amplify inflammatory response

**Fatty Streak Formation:**
- Accumulation of foam cells in intima
- **Visible as yellow streaks/spots** in arteries
- **Can begin in childhood/adolescence**
- **Reversible** at this stage if risk factors removed

#### Stage 4: Plaque Progression and Complication

**Smooth Muscle Cell Migration and Proliferation:**
- Growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) released by macrophages, platelets
- Smooth muscle cells migrate from media to intima
- Proliferate and produce extracellular matrix:
  - **Collagen**
  - **Elastin**
  - **Proteoglycans**

**Fibrous Cap Formation:**
- Smooth muscle cells and collagen form **fibrous cap** over lipid core
- **Fibrous cap** covers and stabilizes plaque
- Separates thrombogenic lipid core from bloodstream

**Lipid Core (Necrotic Core):**
- Central area of plaque
- Contains:
  - Foam cells (dead and dying)
  - Extracellular lipid
  - Cellular debris
  - Cholesterol crystals
- **Necrotic** (cell death via apoptosis and necrosis)
- **Highly thrombogenic** if exposed to blood

**Advanced Plaque Structure:**
- **Fibrous cap**: Collagen-rich, smooth muscle cells
- **Shoulder regions**: Edges of plaque; inflammatory cells accumulate (macrophages, T-cells)
- **Lipid core**: Necrotic, acellular, cholesterol-rich
- **Base**: Adjacent to media

**Plaque Progression:**
- **Stable plaque**:
  - Thick fibrous cap
  - Small lipid core
  - Less inflammation
  - Gradual narrowing of lumen (stenosis)
  - May cause **stable angina** (exertional chest pain)
- **Vulnerable (unstable) plaque**:
  - **Thin fibrous cap** (<65 μm)
  - **Large lipid core** (>40% of plaque volume)
  - **Active inflammation** (macrophages in cap and shoulder)
  - Prone to rupture
  - **Most dangerous**: May not cause significant stenosis but high risk of rupture → acute thrombosis

#### Stage 5: Plaque Complications

**1. Plaque Rupture (Most Common Acute Event):**

**Mechanism:**
- **Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)**: Produced by activated macrophages
  - **MMP-1, MMP-2, MMP-9**: Degrade collagen in fibrous cap
- Thin, weakened cap ruptures
- **Lipid core** exposed to bloodstream

**Consequences:**
- **Thrombosis**: Platelets adhere to exposed collagen and tissue factor
- **Platelet activation** → release of TXA₂, ADP → more platelet aggregation
- **Coagulation cascade activation** → thrombin → fibrin clot
- **Thrombus** may:
  - **Occlude artery** → **Acute myocardial infarction (MI)**, stroke
  - **Partially occlude** → **Unstable angina**
  - **Embolize** → travel downstream, block smaller vessels

**Risk Factors for Rupture:**
- Inflammation (↑ CRP, ↑ macrophages)
- Thin fibrous cap
- Large lipid core
- Hemodynamic stress (hypertension)
- Increased sympathetic activity (stress, cocaine)

**2. Plaque Erosion:**
- Loss of endothelial cells over plaque (without rupture)
- Thrombogenic subendothelial matrix exposed
- Thrombus forms
- **More common in women and smokers**

**3. Calcification:**
- Calcium deposition in plaque (dystrophic calcification)
- Advanced plaques become hard, brittle
- ↓ Arterial compliance → ↑ systolic BP
- Visible on CT scan (calcium score predicts CV risk)

**4. Hemorrhage into Plaque:**
- Rupture of vasa vasorum (new blood vessels in plaque)
- Blood leaks into plaque
- Sudden plaque expansion
- May trigger thrombosis

**5. Stenosis (Narrowing):**
- Plaque grows inward → ↓ lumen diameter
- **Hemodynamically significant**: ≥70% diameter stenosis (≥90% cross-sectional area)
- **Stable angina**: Chest pain with exertion (demand exceeds supply)

### Clinical Manifestations

**Depend on Affected Artery:**

**Coronary Arteries:**
- **Stable angina pectoris** (chronic, stable plaque)
- **Acute coronary syndromes** (plaque rupture):
  - Unstable angina
  - Non-ST elevation MI (NSTEMI)
  - ST elevation MI (STEMI)
- **Sudden cardiac death**

**Carotid and Cerebral Arteries:**
- **Transient ischemic attack (TIA)**
- **Ischemic stroke** (thrombotic or embolic)

**Peripheral Arteries (Legs):**
- **Intermittent claudication** (leg pain with walking)
- **Critical limb ischemia**
- **Gangrene**, amputation

**Renal Arteries:**
- **Renovascular hypertension**
- **Chronic kidney disease**

**Mesenteric Arteries:**
- **Mesenteric ischemia** (abdominal pain after eating)

**Aorta:**
- **Aortic aneurysm**
- **Aortic dissection**

### Risk Factors

**Non-Modifiable:**
- **Age**: Men ≥45, women ≥55
- **Male sex**: Men > women until menopause; then equal
- **Family history**: First-degree relative with premature CVD (men <55, women <65)
- **Genetics**: Familial hypercholesterolemia, other genetic dyslipid emias

**Modifiable (Major):**
- **Hyperlipidemia**: Elevated LDL-C, low HDL-C, elevated triglycerides
- **Hypertension**: Accelerates atherosclerosis
- **Diabetes mellitus**: Hyperglycemia, AGEs, dyslipidemia
- **Smoking**: #1 modifiable risk factor; ↑ risk 2-4x
- **Obesity**: Especially visceral adiposity
- **Physical inactivity**
- **Unhealthy diet**: High saturated fat, trans fat, refined carbs

**Emerging Risk Factors:**
- **Chronic inflammation**: Elevated CRP, IL-6
- **Hyperhomocysteinemia**: ↓ B vitamins (folate, B6, B12)
- **Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)]**: Genetic; LDL-like particle with additional apolipoprotein(a)
- **Chronic kidney disease**
- **Autoimmune diseases**: Rheumatoid arthritis, SLE
- **Obstructive sleep apnea**
- **Psychosocial stress, depression**
- **Poor sleep quality**

### Diagnosis and Assessment

**Lipid Panel (Fasting):**
- **Total cholesterol**: Desirable <200 mg/dL
- **LDL cholesterol**: Optimal <100 mg/dL; <70 if high risk
- **HDL cholesterol**: ≥40 mg/dL (men), ≥50 mg/dL (women); higher is better
- **Triglycerides**: <150 mg/dL
- **Non-HDL cholesterol** (Total - HDL): Target <130 mg/dL

**Inflammatory Markers:**
- **High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP)**:
  - Low risk: <1 mg/L
  - Intermediate: 1-3 mg/L
  - High risk: >3 mg/L

**Imaging:**
- **Coronary calcium score** (CT): Quantifies coronary calcification
- **Carotid ultrasound**: Measures intima-media thickness (IMT), detects plaques
- **Angiography** (coronary, carotid): Visualizes stenosis
- **Stress testing**: Assess functional significance of stenosis

# Atherosclerotic Disease {#atherosclerotic-disease}

## Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) {#cad}

### Definition

**Coronary Artery Disease** is atherosclerosis of the coronary arteries, resulting in reduced blood flow to the myocardium (heart muscle). It is the **most common type of heart disease** and leading cause of death in developed countries.

**Synonyms:** Coronary heart disease (CHD), ischemic heart disease (IHD)

### Coronary Artery Anatomy

**Three Main Coronary Arteries:**

**1. Left Anterior Descending (LAD):**
- Supplies: Anterior wall of left ventricle, interventricular septum
- **"Widowmaker"**: LAD occlusion → large anterior MI, often fatal

**2. Left Circumflex (LCx):**
- Supplies: Lateral and posterior walls of left ventricle

**3. Right Coronary Artery (RCA):**
- Supplies: Right ventricle, inferior wall of left ventricle, posterior septum
- Gives rise to: Posterior descending artery (PDA) in right-dominant circulation (85% of people)

**Coronary Perfusion:**
- Occurs primarily during **diastole** (heart relaxation)
- Systole → ventricular contraction → compression of intramyocardial vessels → minimal flow
- **Tachycardia** (rapid heart rate) → shorter diastole → ↓ coronary perfusion time

### Pathophysiology

**Underlying Cause:** Atherosclerosis (see [Atherosclerosis section](#atherosclerosis) for detailed mechanisms)

**Progression:**
1. **Endothelial dysfunction** → ↑ permeability
2. **LDL infiltration and oxidation** → foam cells
3. **Plaque formation** → progressive stenosis
4. **Myocardial ischemia** when stenosis ≥70% (supply cannot meet demand)
5. **Plaque rupture/erosion** → thrombosis → acute coronary syndromes

**Myocardial Oxygen Supply vs Demand:**

**Supply Determined By:**
- **Coronary blood flow**:
  - Perfusion pressure (aortic diastolic pressure - left ventricular end-diastolic pressure)
  - Coronary artery diameter (stenosis ↓ flow)
  - Diastolic time (heart rate)
- **Oxygen-carrying capacity**: Hemoglobin, oxygen saturation

**Demand Determined By:**
- **Heart rate**: ↑ HR → ↑ O₂ demand
- **Contractility**: ↑ Contractility → ↑ O₂ demand
- **Wall stress**:
  - Preload (ventricular volume)
  - Afterload (systolic BP, aortic pressure)
  - Wall thickness

**Ischemia occurs when demand > supply**

### Clinical Manifestations

#### Stable Angina Pectoris (Chronic Stable CAD)

**Definition:** Predictable chest pain triggered by exertion or stress, relieved by rest or nitroglycerin

**Mechanism:**
- Fixed coronary stenosis (typically 70-90%)
- At rest: Supply = demand → no symptoms
- With exertion: ↑ Demand exceeds fixed supply → ischemia → angina

**Classic Angina Characteristics:**
- **Location**: Substernal (behind sternum), may radiate to:
  - Left arm (most common)
  - Jaw
  - Neck
  - Back
  - Right arm
- **Quality**: Pressure, tightness, squeezing, heaviness, "elephant sitting on chest"
  - **NOT sharp, stabbing, or pleuritic** (if so, less likely cardiac)
- **Duration**: 2-10 minutes (typically <20 minutes)
- **Triggers**: Physical exertion, emotional stress, cold weather, heavy meals
- **Relief**: Rest (2-5 minutes), nitroglycerin (1-3 minutes)

**Anginal Equivalents** (especially in elderly, diabetics, women):
- **Dyspnea** (shortness of breath) without chest pain
- **Fatigue**
- **Nausea**
- **Dizziness**

**Canadian Cardiovascular Society (CCS) Angina Grading:**

| Class | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **I** | Angina only with strenuous/prolonged exertion; ordinary activity does not cause angina |
| **II** | Slight limitation: Angina with walking >2 blocks on level ground, climbing >1 flight of stairs at normal pace |
| **III** | Marked limitation: Angina with walking 1-2 blocks, climbing 1 flight of stairs |
| **IV** | Inability to perform any physical activity without angina; may have angina at rest |

**Stable Angina Diagnosis:**

**Clinical Diagnosis:** History is key

**Confirm Ischemia:**
- **Exercise Stress Test (EST)**:
  - ECG monitoring during treadmill exercise
  - **Positive**: ST-segment depression ≥1 mm, chest pain, hypotension
  - **Sensitivity**: ~70%, **Specificity**: ~70%
- **Stress Echocardiography**:
  - Detect wall motion abnormalities with stress (exercise or dobutamine)
  - Higher sensitivity/specificity than EST alone
- **Nuclear Stress Test** (myocardial perfusion imaging):
  - Inject radiotracer (Tc-99m sestamibi, thallium-201)
  - Images at rest and with stress
  - **Defect with stress, normal at rest** → reversible ischemia
  - High sensitivity/specificity (~85-90%)

**Anatomic Assessment:**
- **Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)**:
  - Non-invasive visualization of coronary arteries
  - Excellent negative predictive value (rules out CAD)
- **Invasive Coronary Angiography**:
  - **Gold standard** for assessing stenosis
  - Cardiac catheterization with contrast injection
  - Identifies location and severity of stenosis
  - **Hemodynamically significant**: ≥70% stenosis (≥50% for left main)

**Fractional Flow Reserve (FFR):**
- Measured during angiography
- Assesses functional significance of stenosis
- FFR ≤0.80 → ischemia-causing lesion → benefits from revascularization

#### Acute Coronary Syndromes (ACS)

**Definition:** Spectrum of conditions caused by **acute myocardial ischemia** due to **plaque rupture/erosion → thrombosis**

**Three Types:**
1. **Unstable Angina (UA)**
2. **Non-ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction (NSTEMI)**
3. **ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)**

**Distinguishing Features:**

| Feature | Unstable Angina | NSTEMI | STEMI |
|---------|-----------------|--------|-------|
| **ECG** | ST depression, T-wave inversion, or normal | ST depression, T-wave inversion | **ST elevation** ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads |
| **Cardiac Biomarkers** (Troponin) | **Normal** | **Elevated** | **Elevated** |
| **Thrombus** | Partial occlusion | Partial occlusion | **Complete occlusion** |
| **Myocardial Necrosis** | None | Yes (small) | Yes (large) |

**Pathophysiology:** See [Myocardial Infarction section](#mi) for detailed MI mechanisms

**Unstable Angina Characteristics:**
- **Crescendo pattern**: Increasing frequency, severity, or duration
- **New-onset severe angina** (<2 months)
- **Angina at rest** (or with minimal exertion)
- **Post-MI angina** (within 2 weeks of MI)
- Troponin **normal** (no myocardial necrosis yet)

**Management:**
- **Medical emergency**: Hospitalization, telemetry monitoring
- **Antiplatelet therapy**: Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor)
- **Anticoagulation**: Heparin (unfractionated or LMWH)
- **Anti-ischemic therapy**: Beta-blockers, nitrates
- **Early invasive strategy**: Angiography within 24-72 hours → revascularization (PCI or CABG) if indicated

### Treatment of Stable CAD

**Goals:**
1. **Relieve symptoms** (angina)
2. **Prevent MI and death** (risk reduction)
3. **Improve quality of life**

#### Medical Management

**Antiplatelet Therapy:**
- **Aspirin 81 mg daily**: ↓ MI, stroke, cardiovascular death by 25%
  - Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → ↓ TXA₂ → ↓ platelet aggregation
  - **Cornerstone of CAD therapy**

**Lipid-Lowering Therapy:**
- **Statins** (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors):
  - **High-intensity statin**: Atorvastatin 40-80 mg, rosuvastatin 20-40 mg
  - **Goal**: LDL-C <70 mg/dL (very high risk: <55 mg/dL)
  - **Benefits**: ↓ LDL-C, plaque stabilization, ↓ inflammation, ↓ CV events by 30-40%
  - **Proven mortality benefit** in secondary prevention
- **Ezetimibe**: Add if LDL not at goal on statin
- **PCSK9 inhibitors**: Evolocumab, alirocumab (injectable; very high risk or familial hypercholesterolemia)

**Anti-Ischemic Therapy (Symptom Relief):**

**1. Beta-Blockers:**
- Examples: Metoprolol, carvedilol, atenolol
- **Mechanisms**:
  - ↓ Heart rate → ↓ O₂ demand, ↑ diastolic time (↑ coronary perfusion)
  - ↓ Contractility → ↓ O₂ demand
  - ↓ BP → ↓ afterload
- **Indications**: First-line for angina; **mandatory** post-MI
- **Benefits**: ↓ Angina frequency, ↓ mortality post-MI

**2. Nitrates:**
- **Sublingual nitroglycerin**: 0.4 mg as needed for angina (onset 1-3 min)
  - Repeat every 5 min x 3 doses
  - **If no relief after 3 doses → call 911** (possible MI)
- **Long-acting nitrates**: Isosorbide mononitrate (for chronic angina)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Vasodilation (veins > arteries)
  - ↓ Preload → ↓ wall stress → ↓ O₂ demand
  - Coronary vasodilation → ↑ supply
- **Side effects**: Headache, hypotension
- **Tolerance**: Requires nitrate-free interval (10-12 hours) to prevent tolerance

**3. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs):**
- **Non-dihydropyridines** (diltiazem, verapamil): ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, vasodilation
- **Dihydropyridines** (amlodipine): Vasodilation (↓ afterload)
- **Indication**: Alternative to beta-blockers if contraindicated; add-on for refractory angina

**4. Ranolazine:**
- Late sodium current inhibitor
- Anti-anginal without affecting HR or BP
- Add-on therapy for refractory angina

**ACE Inhibitors or ARBs:**
- **Indications**: CAD + hypertension, diabetes, LV dysfunction, or prior MI
- **Benefits**: ↓ Afterload, prevent LV remodeling, renoprotective

#### Revascularization

**Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI):**
- **Procedure**: Cardiac catheterization → balloon angioplasty ± stent placement
- **Indications**:
  - Refractory angina despite medical therapy
  - High-risk anatomy on stress test
  - Hemodynamically significant stenosis (FFR ≤0.80)
- **Types of Stents**:
  - **Bare-metal stents (BMS)**: Higher restenosis rate
  - **Drug-eluting stents (DES)**: Coated with antiproliferative drugs (sirolimus, paclitaxel); ↓ restenosis
- **Post-PCI**: Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) - aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor x 6-12 months (longer for DES)

**Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG):**
- **Procedure**: Surgical bypass of stenotic segments using grafts
  - **Arterial grafts**: Left internal mammary artery (LIMA) → LAD (best patency)
  - **Venous grafts**: Saphenous vein
- **Indications**:
  - **Left main disease**
  - **Three-vessel disease**
  - **Two-vessel disease with proximal LAD stenosis**
  - **Diabetes + multivessel disease** (CABG > PCI)
- **Benefits**: Superior long-term outcomes for complex multivessel disease

## Myocardial Infarction (MI) {#mi}

### Definition

**Myocardial Infarction** (heart attack) is **irreversible myocardial cell death (necrosis)** due to prolonged ischemia. It is a **medical emergency** with high mortality.

**Diagnosis:** Requires ≥2 of:
1. **Cardiac biomarker elevation** (troponin)
2. **Clinical symptoms** (chest pain)
3. **ECG changes** (ST elevation, new Q waves)
4. **Imaging evidence** of new loss of viable myocardium

### Types of MI

**Type 1 MI (Atherosclerotic Plaque Disruption - Most Common ~70%):**
- **Mechanism**: Plaque rupture or erosion → **thrombosis** → coronary occlusion
- **"Spontaneous" MI**

**Type 2 MI (Supply-Demand Mismatch):**
- **Mechanism**: Ischemia due to imbalance between O₂ supply and demand
- **NOT due to acute thrombosis**
- **Causes**:
  - Severe hypotension (shock, sepsis)
  - Severe hypertension (↑↑ demand)
  - Tachyarrhythmia (↓ diastolic perfusion time)
  - Severe anemia (↓ O₂ carrying capacity)
  - Respiratory failure (hypoxemia)
  - Coronary vasospasm
  - Coronary dissection

**Type 3, 4, 5 MI:** Procedural (PCI-related, CABG-related)

### Pathophysiology of Type 1 MI (Plaque Rupture → Thrombosis)

#### Stage 1: Plaque Rupture/Erosion

**Vulnerable Plaque Characteristics:**
- **Thin fibrous cap** (<65 μm)
- **Large lipid core** (>40% of plaque)
- **Active inflammation**: Macrophages secrete matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) → degrade collagen cap

**Rupture Triggers:**
- **Hemodynamic stress**: Acute hypertension, sympathetic surge
- **Circadian rhythm**: Peak MI incidence 6-10 AM (↑ sympathetic activity, ↑ cortisol, ↑ platelet aggregability)
- **Physical exertion**: Especially in sedentary individuals
- **Emotional stress**
- **Cocaine use**: Intense sympathetic stimulation, coronary vasospasm

**Result:** Lipid core exposed to bloodstream

#### Stage 2: Thrombosis

**Immediate Platelet Response:**
- **Platelet adhesion**: Bind to exposed collagen (via GPIb receptor and von Willebrand factor)
- **Platelet activation**: Release of:
  - **ADP**: Amplifies platelet aggregation (P2Y12 receptor activation)
  - **Thromboxane A₂ (TXA₂)**: Potent platelet aggregator and vasoconstrictor
  - **Serotonin**: Vasoconstrictor
- **Platelet aggregation**: Activated platelets bind via GPIIb/IIIa receptors + fibrinogen → platelet plug

**Coagulation Cascade Activation:**
- **Tissue factor** (abundant in lipid core) initiates extrinsic pathway
- **Factor VII** → **Factor X** → **Factor II (prothrombin)** → **Thrombin**
- **Thrombin**: Converts fibrinogen → **fibrin** → stabilizes platelet plug
- **Thrombus** formation: Platelet plug + fibrin mesh

**Extent of Thrombosis:**
- **Complete occlusion** → **STEMI** (ST-elevation MI)
  - Large thrombus completely blocks artery
  - **Transmural infarction** (full thickness of myocardial wall)
  - **Large MI**, higher mortality
- **Partial occlusion** or **distal embolization** → **NSTEMI** (non-ST-elevation MI)
  - Incomplete obstruction
  - **Subendocardial infarction** (inner layer of myocardium)
  - **Smaller MI**, but still serious

#### Stage 3: Myocardial Ischemia and Necrosis

**Timeline of Ischemia:**
- **<20 minutes**: **Reversible injury** (if reperfusion occurs)
  - Cellular energy depletion (↓ ATP)
  - Anaerobic metabolism → lactic acid accumulation
  - Electrical dysfunction (arrhythmias) but cells viable
- **>20-30 minutes**: **Irreversible injury** → **cell death (necrosis)**
  - Cell membrane disruption
  - Release of intracellular contents (troponin, CK-MB)
- **First area of necrosis**: **Subendocardium** (innermost layer)
  - Most vulnerable (furthest from epicardial blood supply, highest wall stress)
- **Wave front of necrosis**: Extends from subendocardium → epicardium over 3-6 hours
- **Complete infarction**: If no reperfusion, entire territory of occluded artery becomes necrotic by 6-12 hours

**Metabolic Consequences:**
- ↓ ATP → failure of Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase → Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ accumulate intracellularly
- ↑ Intracellular Ca²⁺ → hypercontracture → cell death
- Mitochondrial dysfunction
- Free radical formation

**Cellular Necrosis:**
- **Coagulative necrosis**: Cell outlines preserved initially
- Release of cardiac biomarkers: **Troponin** (TnI, TnT), CK-MB

#### Stage 4: Inflammatory Response and Healing

**Acute Phase (0-4 days):**
- **Neutrophil infiltration** (24-48 hours)
- Clear necrotic debris
- **Soft, vulnerable myocardium** → risk of **rupture** (days 3-7)

**Reparative Phase (5-14 days):**
- **Macrophages** replace neutrophils
- **Granulation tissue** formation (new blood vessels, fibroblasts)
- Necrotic tissue cleared

**Maturation Phase (2 weeks - 2 months):**
- **Scar formation**: Collagen deposition
- **Fibrous scar replaces necrotic myocardium** (completed by 6-8 weeks)
- Scar is **non-contractile** → ↓ ventricular function

**Ventricular Remodeling:**
- **Infarct expansion**: Thinning and dilation of infarcted segment (first few days)
- **Global LV remodeling**: Non-infarcted segments hypertrophy (compensatory)
- **LV dilation**: Over weeks to months
- **May progress to heart failure**

### Clinical Presentation

**Classic MI Symptoms:**
- **Chest pain/discomfort**:
  - **Substernal** (behind breastbone)
  - **Quality**: Crushing, pressure, squeezing, heaviness, tightness
  - **Radiation**: Left arm, jaw, neck, back, epigastrium
  - **Duration**: >20 minutes (unlike stable angina <10 min)
  - **NOT relieved by rest or nitroglycerin** (unlike stable angina)
  - **Severity**: Often described as "worst pain of my life"
- **Associated symptoms**:
  - **Dyspnea** (shortness of breath)
  - **Diaphoresis** (profuse sweating)
  - **Nausea/vomiting**
  - **Lightheadedness, syncope**
  - **Anxiety**, sense of impending doom
  - **Palpitations**

**Atypical Presentations (More Common in Women, Elderly, Diabetics):**
- **No chest pain**: 20-30% of MIs (especially diabetics - "silent MI")
- **Dyspnea** as primary symptom
- **Epigastric pain** ("indigestion")
- **Fatigue, weakness**
- **Isolated arm, jaw, or back pain**
- **Confusion** (elderly)

**Physical Exam:**
- Often unremarkable
- **Diaphoresis** (sweating)
- **Pallor**
- **Tachycardia** or **bradycardia**
- **Hypotension** (if cardiogenic shock)
- **S4 gallop** (stiff ventricle)
- **New murmur** (papillary muscle rupture → mitral regurgitation)
- **Crackles** (pulmonary edema if LV failure)
- **Jugular venous distension** (if RV infarction)

### Diagnosis

**ECG (Immediate - <10 minutes of arrival):**

**STEMI:**
- **ST-segment elevation** ≥1 mm (0.1 mV) in ≥2 contiguous leads
  - **Contiguous leads** define coronary territory:
    - **Anterior MI** (LAD): V1-V4 (may extend to V5-V6, I, aVL)
    - **Inferior MI** (RCA): II, III, aVF
    - **Lateral MI** (LCx): I, aVL, V5-V6
    - **Posterior MI**: ST depression V1-V3 (mirror image); **need posterior leads** (V7-V9) for ST elevation
- **New LBBB** (left bundle branch block): Treated as STEMI equivalent
- **Hyperacute T waves** (tall, peaked): Very early sign (minutes)

**NSTEMI/Unstable Angina:**
- **ST depression** (≥0.5 mm)
- **T-wave inversion** (≥1 mm)
- **Non-specific changes**
- **Normal ECG** (15-20% of cases): Does NOT rule out MI

**Evolution of ECG in STEMI:**
- **Minutes-hours**: Hyperacute T waves → ST elevation
- **Hours-days**: ST elevation persists; T waves invert; Q waves appear
- **Days-weeks**: ST normalizes; Q waves persist (evidence of old MI)

**Cardiac Biomarkers:**

**Troponin (TnI or TnT) - Gold Standard:**
- **Highly sensitive and specific** for myocardial injury
- **Detectable**: 3-6 hours after onset
- **Peak**: 12-24 hours
- **Duration**: 7-14 days (prolonged elevation)
- **High-sensitivity troponin (hs-Tn)**:
  - Detects even minor myocardial injury
  - Can diagnose MI as early as 1-3 hours
  - Serial measurements: 0 hours and 1-3 hours
  - **Criteria**: Rise and/or fall of troponin with ≥1 value above 99th percentile + clinical evidence of ischemia

**Creatine Kinase-MB (CK-MB):**
- Less sensitive/specific than troponin
- Rises 3-6 hours, peaks 12-24 hours, normalizes 48-72 hours
- Useful for detecting **reinfarction** (troponin remains elevated for days)

**Myoglobin:**
- Earliest marker (1-3 hours) but non-specific (also elevated in skeletal muscle injury)
- Rarely used clinically

**Imaging:**

**Echocardiography:**
- Detects **wall motion abnormalities** (hypokinesis, akinesis, dyskinesis) in affected territory
- Assesses **LV function** (ejection fraction)
- Identifies **complications**: Papillary muscle rupture, VSD, LV thrombus, pericardial effusion

**Coronary Angiography:**
- **Performed emergently** in STEMI (primary PCI)
- Identifies **culprit lesion**
- Guides revascularization

### Complications of MI

**Immediate (Hours to Days):**

**1. Arrhythmias (Most Common Complication):**
- **Ventricular fibrillation (VF)**: Cause of sudden cardiac death; peaks in first 4 hours
- **Ventricular tachycardia (VT)**
- **Accelerated idioventricular rhythm (AIVR)**: "Reperfusion arrhythmia" (benign)
- **Bradyarrhythmias**: Sinus bradycardia, AV blocks (especially inferior MI - RCA supplies AV node)
- **Atrial fibrillation**: 10-20% of MIs

**2. Cardiogenic Shock:**
- **Definition**: ↓ Cardiac output + hypotension + end-organ hypoperfusion
- **Causes**: Massive MI (>40% LV), mechanical complications
- **Mortality**: 40-50%
- **Treatment**: Inotropes, mechanical circulatory support (IABP, Impella), urgent revascularization

**3. Heart Failure / Pulmonary Edema:**
- LV dysfunction → ↓ cardiac output, ↑ LV pressure → pulmonary congestion
- **Killip Classification**:
  - Class I: No heart failure (mortality ~5%)
  - Class II: Mild heart failure (crackles, S3) (mortality ~15%)
  - Class III: Pulmonary edema (mortality ~30-40%)
  - Class IV: Cardiogenic shock (mortality ~50%)

**Early (Days):**

**4. Mechanical Complications (Days 3-7 - During "Soft" Necrotic Phase):**
- **Free wall rupture**:
  - Tear through LV wall → hemopericardium → **cardiac tamponade** → death
  - Mortality >90%
- **Ventricular septal defect (VSD)**:
  - Rupture of interventricular septum
  - Left-to-right shunt → acute heart failure
  - **New harsh holosystolic murmur** at left sternal border
  - Mortality 50-90% without surgery
- **Papillary muscle rupture**:
  - Causes acute **severe mitral regurgitation**
  - **New holosystolic murmur** at apex → radiates to axilla
  - Acute pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock
  - **Posteromedial papillary muscle** (supplied only by RCA) most vulnerable
  - Mortality 50-90% without surgery

**5. Pericarditis (Acute):**
- **Fibrinous pericarditis**: Inflammation of pericardium overlying infarct
- **Timing**: 2-4 days post-MI
- **Symptoms**: Sharp, pleuritic chest pain, worse with lying flat, relieved by sitting forward
- **Pericardial friction rub** on exam
- **Treatment**: NSAIDs, colchicine (avoid in first 24 hours post-MI)

**Late (Weeks to Months):**

**6. Dressler Syndrome (Post-MI Syndrome):**
- **Autoimmune pericarditis**: Antibodies against cardiac antigens
- **Timing**: 2-10 weeks post-MI (peak 3-4 weeks)
- **Symptoms**: Fever, pleuritic chest pain, pericardial effusion, pleural effusion
- **Treatment**: NSAIDs, colchicine, corticosteroids if refractory

**7. LV Thrombus:**
- Forms on akinetic/dyskinetic segment (especially anterior MI, apical aneurysm)
- Risk of **systemic embolization** → stroke
- **Anticoagulation** (warfarin x 3-6 months) if thrombus detected

**8. Ventricular Aneurysm:**
- Outpouching of thin scar tissue (usually anterior wall)
- **Persistent ST elevation** on ECG (weeks after MI)
- Complications: Arrhythmias, heart failure, LV thrombus
- Surgery if large, symptomatic

**9. Chronic Heart Failure:**
- LV remodeling → progressive dilation, ↓ EF
- See [Heart Failure section](#chf)

### Treatment of STEMI (Time-Critical Emergency)

**"Time is Muscle"** - Every minute of delay → more myocardium dies

**Goals:**
1. **Rapid reperfusion** (restore blood flow)
2. **Limit infarct size**
3. **Prevent complications**

**Immediate Management (First Medical Contact):**

**"MONA" + Antiplatelet + Anticoagulation:**

**1. Morphine:**
- For **severe pain** (use judiciously)
- Reduces sympathetic activation, anxiety
- **Caution**: Associated with ↑ mortality in some studies; use only if needed

**2. Oxygen:**
- **Only if hypoxemic** (SpO₂ <90%)
- Routine oxygen NOT beneficial (may be harmful)

**3. Nitroglycerin:**
- Sublingual 0.4 mg every 5 min x 3 doses (if ongoing chest pain, BP >90 systolic)
- Then IV nitroglycerin
- **Contraindications**: RV infarction, hypotension, recent phosphodiesterase inhibitor (Viagra, Cialis)

**4. Aspirin:**
- **162-325 mg chewed immediately** (faster absorption)
- **Most important acute intervention** (↓ mortality by 23%)
- Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → ↓ TXA₂ → ↓ platelet aggregation

**5. P2Y12 Inhibitor (Second Antiplatelet):**
- **Clopidogrel** (Plavix): 600 mg loading dose
- **Ticagrelor** (Brilinta): 180 mg loading dose (preferred - more potent)
- **Prasugrel** (Effient): 60 mg loading dose
- **Mechanism**: Blocks ADP receptor on platelets

**6. Anticoagulation:**
- **Unfractionated heparin (UFH)**: Bolus + infusion
- **OR Low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH)**: Enoxaparin
- Prevents thrombus propagation

**7. Beta-Blocker:**
- **Oral metoprolol** within 24 hours (if no contraindications)
- ↓ Myocardial O₂ demand, ↓ arrhythmias, ↓ infarct size
- **Contraindications**: Hypotension, bradycardia, heart failure, shock

**Reperfusion Therapy (Restore Blood Flow):**

**Goal: Door-to-Balloon Time <90 minutes (PCI) or Door-to-Needle Time <30 minutes (Fibrinolysis)**

**Primary PCI (Percutaneous Coronary Intervention) - Preferred if Available:**
- Emergency cardiac catheterization → balloon angioplasty ± stent of culprit lesion
- **Advantages**:
  - **Superior outcomes**: ↓ Mortality, ↓ reinfarction, ↓ stroke vs fibrinolysis
  - **Can be performed even if delayed presentation** (>12 hours)
  - Identifies coronary anatomy
- **Disadvantages**: Requires cardiac catheterization lab, 24/7 availability
- **Goal**: <90 minutes from first medical contact to balloon inflation (<60 min if hospital transfer)

**Fibrinolytic Therapy (If PCI Not Available Within 120 Minutes):**
- IV thrombolytic (tPA, reteplase, tenecteplase)
- **Mechanism**: Activates plasminogen → plasmin → cleaves fibrin → dissolves clot
- **Most effective**: If given within **3 hours** of symptom onset (earlier = better)
- **Benefit up to 12 hours**
- **Contraindications**:
  - Prior ICH (intracranial hemorrhage)
  - Ischemic stroke within 3 months
  - Active bleeding
  - Recent surgery
  - Suspected aortic dissection
- **Complications**: Bleeding (ICH in 0.5-1%), allergic reactions
- **After fibrinolysis**: Transfer for angiography (rescue PCI if failed reperfusion)

**Signs of Successful Reperfusion:**
- Resolution of chest pain
- ST-segment normalization (50-70% reduction)
- Reperfusion arrhythmias (AIVR - accelerated idioventricular rhythm)
- Early peak of cardiac biomarkers ("washout")

**Post-MI Medical Therapy (Secondary Prevention):**

**"ABCDE":**
- **A**spirin (81 mg daily indefinitely) + **A**ntiplatelet (P2Y12 inhibitor x 12 months)
- **B**eta-blocker (indefinitely)
- **C**holesterol management (high-intensity statin; LDL goal <70)
- **D**iet and **D**iabetes management
- **E**xercise and **E**ducation

**ACE Inhibitor or ARB:**
- **Start within 24 hours** if anterior MI, LV dysfunction (EF <40%), heart failure, hypertension, diabetes
- ↓ LV remodeling, ↓ mortality

**Aldosterone Antagonist:**
- **Spironolactone or eplerenone**: If EF ≤40% + heart failure or diabetes
- ↓ Mortality in post-MI LV dysfunction

### Herbal Considerations Post-MI

**CRITICAL:** MI patients require intensive medical management. Herbs are **not primary therapy**. Any herbal interventions must be **coordinated with cardiologist**.

**Post-MI Recovery Support (After Acute Phase, During Cardiac Rehab):**

**1. Coenzyme Q10:**
- **Dose**: 200-400 mg daily
- **Rationale**: Supports myocardial energy metabolism; depleted by statins
- **Evidence**: May improve outcomes in heart failure
- **Mandatory** if on statin

**2. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA):**
- **Dose**: 2-4g daily
- **Rationale**: Anti-inflammatory, anti-arrhythmic, plaque stabilization
- **Evidence**: ↓ Cardiovascular events in post-MI patients (some trials)
- **Safe** with aspirin/P2Y12 inhibitor

**3. Magnesium:**
- **Dose**: 400-600 mg daily (if not contraindicated)
- **Rationale**: Anti-arrhythmic; deficiency common
- **Caution**: Monitor if renal dysfunction

**4. L-Carnitine:**
- **Dose**: 2-3g daily
- **Rationale**: Improves myocardial energy metabolism, ↓ infarct size (animal studies)
- **Evidence**: Some studies show benefit post-MI; more research needed

**5. Hawthorn:**
- **Dose**: 450-900 mg daily
- **Rationale**: Cardioprotective, improves cardiac output
- **Timing**: Start after acute phase (weeks post-MI) with cardiology approval
- **Avoid acutely** (no evidence for acute MI; focus on proven therapies)

**Herbs to AVOID Post-MI:**
- **Ma Huang (Ephedra)**: Sympathomimetic, ↑ BP, ↑ HR, arrhythmogenic - CONTRAINDICATED
- **Yohimbe**: Sympathomimetic - CONTRAINDICATED
- **Licorice** (large amounts): ↑ BP, hypokalemia
- **Grapefruit juice**: Inhibits CYP3A4 → ↑ statin levels → ↑ toxicity

**Clinical Note:** **DO NOT use herbs as alternatives to proven post-MI therapies** (aspirin, P2Y12 inhibitor, beta-blocker, statin, ACE-I). Herbs can provide supportive benefits during recovery phase. CoQ10 and omega-3 have best evidence. Emphasize **cardiac rehabilitation**, lifestyle modification, medication adherence - these save lives.

# Heart Failure {#heart-failure}

## Congestive Heart Failure (CHF) {#chf}

### Definition

**Heart Failure** is a clinical syndrome resulting from the heart's inability to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's metabolic demands OR doing so only at elevated filling pressures. It represents the **final common pathway** of many cardiovascular diseases.

**Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)** emphasizes the **fluid retention** (congestion) that often accompanies heart failure.

**Epidemiology:**
- Affects >6 million Americans
- Incidence ↑ with age (≥10% of those >65 years)
- Leading cause of hospitalization in elderly
- 5-year mortality: 50% (worse than many cancers)
- Major public health burden

### Normal Cardiac Function Review

**Cardiac Output (CO)** = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)

**Stroke Volume determined by:**
1. **Preload**: Ventricular end-diastolic volume (filling)
   - Frank-Starling Law: ↑ Preload → ↑ stretch → ↑ contractility → ↑ stroke volume (up to a point)
2. **Afterload**: Resistance heart must overcome to eject blood
   - Determined by systemic vascular resistance (BP)
3. **Contractility**: Intrinsic ability of myocardium to contract

**Ejection Fraction (EF):**
- **EF = (SV / EDV) × 100**
- Normal EF: **50-70%**
- **Reduced EF (HFrEF)**: <40%
- **Preserved EF (HFpEF)**: ≥50%

### Classification Systems

#### New York Heart Association (NYHA) Functional Classification

| Class | Symptoms |
|-------|----------|
| **I** | **No limitation**: Ordinary physical activity does not cause symptoms |
| **II** | **Slight limitation**: Comfortable at rest; ordinary activity causes fatigue, dyspnea, palpitations |
| **III** | **Marked limitation**: Comfortable at rest; less than ordinary activity causes symptoms |
| **IV** | **Symptoms at rest**: Unable to carry out any physical activity without discomfort; symptoms worsen with any activity |

#### ACC/AHA Stages of Heart Failure

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **A** | **At risk**: Hypertension, diabetes, CAD, family history; NO structural heart disease, NO symptoms |
| **B** | **Structural disease**: LV hypertrophy, previous MI, low EF; NO symptoms |
| **C** | **Symptomatic HF**: Structural disease + current or prior HF symptoms |
| **D** | **Refractory/End-stage**: Severe symptoms despite maximal therapy; requires advanced interventions (transplant, VAD, palliative care) |

**Key Difference:** ACC/AHA stages are **progressive** and **irreversible** (cannot go backward); NYHA classes can fluctuate with treatment.

### Types of Heart Failure

#### 1. Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF) - Systolic Dysfunction

**Definition:** **EF <40%** + symptoms of heart failure

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Impaired contractility** → ↓ stroke volume → ↓ cardiac output
- Heart cannot **pump out** blood effectively

**Causes:**
- **Coronary artery disease / MI** (Most common - 60-70%)
  - Myocardial scarring → loss of contractile tissue
- **Dilated cardiomyopathy**:
  - Idiopathic
  - Viral myocarditis (Coxsackievirus, parvovirus B19, HIV)
  - Toxic: Alcohol, cocaine, chemotherapy (doxorubicin, trastuzumab)
  - Peripartum cardiomyopathy
  - Genetic/familial
- **Chronic hypertension** → LVH → eventual systolic dysfunction
- **Valvular disease**: Severe mitral regurgitation, aortic regurgitation
- **Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy**: Chronic rapid heart rate → dysfunction (reversible if rate controlled)

**Hemodynamics:**
- ↓ Ejection fraction (<40%)
- ↓ Cardiac output
- ↑ Left ventricular end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) - backup of blood
- ↑ Left atrial pressure → ↑ pulmonary venous pressure → pulmonary edema

#### 2. Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF) - Diastolic Dysfunction

**Definition:** **EF ≥50%** + symptoms/signs of heart failure + evidence of diastolic dysfunction

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Impaired relaxation** → ↓ ventricular filling → ↓ stroke volume
- Heart is **stiff** (poor compliance), cannot **fill** adequately during diastole
- **Systolic function preserved** (heart contracts normally)

**Causes:**
- **Chronic hypertension** (Most common)
  - LV hypertrophy → stiff, non-compliant ventricle
- **Aging**: Myocardial fibrosis, ↓ compliance
- **Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy**
- **Restrictive cardiomyopathy**:
  - Amyloidosis (protein deposits)
  - Sarcoidosis
  - Hemochromatosis (iron overload)
  - Endomyocardial fibrosis
- **Pericardial disease**: Constrictive pericarditis, tamponade
- **Obesity**: Epicardial fat, ↑ ventricular stiffness
- **Diabetes mellitus**: Myocardial fibrosis

**Hemodynamics:**
- **Normal or near-normal ejection fraction** (≥50%)
- ↑ Left ventricular end-diastolic pressure (LVEDP) - high filling pressures needed
- ↑ Left atrial pressure → pulmonary congestion
- Cardiac output often normal at rest but fails to increase appropriately with exercise

**Clinical Distinction:**
- HFpEF more common in: **Elderly, women, obese, hypertensive, diabetic**
- Symptoms often provoked by **exertion** (inability to ↑ cardiac output with activity)

#### 3. HF with Mildly Reduced EF (HFmrEF)

**Definition:** EF 41-49% + symptoms/signs of HF

**"Gray zone"** - May represent recovering HFrEF or early HFpEF

### Pathophysiology of Heart Failure Progression

#### Stage 1: Initial Insult

**Cardiac injury from:**
- MI → myocardial scarring
- Chronic hypertension → pressure overload
- Valvular disease → volume overload
- Cardiomyopathy → intrinsic myocardial dysfunction

**Result:** ↓ Cardiac output

#### Stage 2: Compensatory Mechanisms (Initially Adaptive, Become Maladaptive)

**Body attempts to maintain blood pressure and perfusion:**

**1. Sympathetic Nervous System Activation:**
- ↓ CO sensed by baroreceptors → ↑ sympathetic output
- **Effects**:
  - ↑ Heart rate (↑ CO)
  - ↑ Contractility (↑ CO)
  - Vasoconstriction (maintain BP)
  - ↑ Renin release
- **Maladaptive consequences**:
  - **↑ Myocardial O₂ demand** → ischemia risk
  - **Arrhythmias** (catecholamine-induced)
  - **Direct myocardial toxicity** (chronic catecholamine exposure → apoptosis, fibrosis)
  - ↑ Afterload → further ↓ CO

**2. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) Activation:**
- ↓ Renal perfusion → ↑ renin
- **Angiotensin II effects**:
  - Vasoconstriction (maintain BP)
  - ↑ Aldosterone → Na⁺ and H₂O retention → ↑ blood volume (↑ preload)
  - Stimulates thirst
  - **Promotes cardiac remodeling**: Myocyte hypertrophy, fibrosis
- **Maladaptive consequences**:
  - **↑ Afterload** (vasoconstriction) → further ↓ CO
  - **Excessive volume retention** → pulmonary/systemic congestion (edema)
  - **Ventricular remodeling** → progressive dysfunction

**3. Ventricular Remodeling:**

**HFrEF (Eccentric Hypertrophy):**
- **Chamber dilation**: Heart stretches to accommodate ↑ volume
- **Wall thinning**
- Initially ↑ stroke volume (Frank-Starling)
- Eventually **excessive dilation** → ↓ contractility (overstretched sarcomeres)
- **Spherical shape** → ↑ wall stress → ↑ O₂ demand, ↓ efficiency

**HFpEF (Concentric Hypertrophy):**
- **Wall thickening** (pressure overload from hypertension)
- **Normal or ↓ chamber size**
- ↓ Compliance → ↑ filling pressures

**Common to Both:**
- **Myocyte hypertrophy**
- **Interstitial fibrosis**: Collagen deposition → stiff myocardium
- **Myocyte apoptosis** (programmed cell death)
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: ↓ Energy production
- Progressive loss of contractile function

**4. Natriuretic Peptides (Counter-regulatory):**
- **BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide)** and **ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide)**
- Released by stretched ventricles and atria
- **Actions**:
  - Natriuresis and diuresis (↓ volume)
  - Vasodilation
  - Inhibit RAAS
- **Beneficial but overwhelmed** in advanced HF
- **BNP levels correlate with HF severity** (diagnostic/prognostic marker)

#### Stage 3: Decompensation (Heart Failure Syndrome)

**Compensatory mechanisms fail:**
- ↓ Cardiac output insufficient to meet demands
- Excessive fluid retention → congestion
- Organ hypoperfusion
- **Vicious cycle**: HF begets more HF

**Clinical Manifestations:**

**Forward Failure** (↓ Cardiac output → organ hypoperfusion):
- Fatigue, weakness
- Renal hypoperfusion → ↓ GFR, ↑ creatinine (cardiorenal syndrome)
- Cerebral hypoperfusion → confusion (especially elderly)
- Cool extremities
- Narrow pulse pressure

**Backward Failure** (Venous congestion):

**Left Heart Failure:**
- ↑ LV pressure → ↑ LA pressure → ↑ pulmonary venous pressure
- **Pulmonary edema**:
  - Fluid leaks from pulmonary capillaries into alveoli
  - **Dyspnea** (shortness of breath) - most common symptom
  - **Orthopnea**: Dyspnea when lying flat (↑ venous return)
  - **Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND)**: Waking at night gasping for air
  - **Crackles (rales)** on lung auscultation
  - **Cough** (may be frothy, pink-tinged sputum in severe pulmonary edema)
  - **Hypoxemia**

**Right Heart Failure:**
- ↑ RV pressure → ↑ RA pressure → ↑ systemic venous pressure
- **Peripheral edema**:
  - Dependent edema (ankles, legs if ambulatory; sacrum if bed-bound)
  - Pitting edema
- **Jugular venous distension (JVD)**: Elevated neck veins
- **Hepatomegaly** (enlarged liver): RUQ discomfort
  - Chronic: **Cardiac cirrhosis** (hepatic fibrosis)
- **Ascites**: Abdominal fluid accumulation
- **Anasarca**: Generalized edema

**Biventricular Failure:** Both left and right heart failure symptoms

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms:**
- **Dyspnea**: Most common; initially with exertion, progresses to rest
- **Orthopnea**: Number of pillows needed to sleep (e.g., "three-pillow orthopnea")
- **Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)**: Waking 2-4 hours after lying down
- **Fatigue, weakness**: Poor perfusion, ↓ O₂ delivery to muscles
- **Exercise intolerance**: ↓ Cardiac output reserve
- **Cough**: Pulmonary congestion; may worsen when lying down
- **Nocturia**: ↑ Renal perfusion when supine at night → ↑ urine production
- **Weight gain**: Fluid retention (2-3 lbs in days)
- **Abdominal distension**: Ascites, hepatomegaly
- **Anorexia, early satiety**: Bowel edema, hepatic congestion

**Physical Exam Signs:**
- **Vital signs**:
  - Tachycardia (compensatory)
  - Tachypnea
  - Hypotension (advanced HF)
  - Narrow pulse pressure
- **Elevated JVP** (jugular venous pressure): >8 cm H₂O
- **Displaced apical impulse**: Lateral and downward (dilated LV)
- **S3 gallop** ("Kentucky"): Rapid ventricular filling; hallmark of HF
- **S4 gallop** ("Tennessee"): Atrial contraction into stiff ventricle (HFpEF)
- **Pulmonary crackles**: Bilateral, basilar (bases of lungs)
- **Wheezing** ("cardiac asthma"): Bronchial edema
- **Peripheral edema**: Pitting, bilateral, dependent
- **Hepatomegaly**: Pulsatile if tricuspid regurgitation
- **Hepatojugular reflux**: Pressure on liver → ↑ JVP
- **Ascites**
- **Cool, clammy extremities**: Poor perfusion
- **Cyanosis**: Hypoxemia (advanced)

### Diagnosis

**Clinical Diagnosis:** Framingham Criteria (2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor)

**Major Criteria:**
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea or orthopnea
- Jugular venous distension
- Pulmonary rales
- S3 gallop
- Cardiomegaly on chest X-ray
- Acute pulmonary edema
- Hepatojugular reflux

**Minor Criteria:**
- Bilateral ankle edema
- Night cough
- Dyspnea on exertion
- Hepatomegaly
- Pleural effusion
- Tachycardia (≥120 bpm)

**Diagnostic Testing:**

**1. B-type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) or NT-proBNP:**
- **BNP >100 pg/mL** or **NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL**: Suggestive of HF
- **Higher levels** → more severe HF
- **Uses**:
  - Differentiate dyspnea from HF vs pulmonary cause
  - Prognostic indicator
  - Monitor treatment response
- **Limitations**: Elevated in renal failure, elderly, women; lower in obesity

**2. Echocardiography (Most Important Test):**
- **Assesses**:
  - **Ejection fraction** (distinguish HFrEF vs HFpEF)
  - **Ventricular size and function**
  - **Wall motion abnormalities** (prior MI)
  - **Valvular abnormalities**
  - **Diastolic function** (E/A ratio, E/e' ratio)
  - **Pulmonary artery pressure**
  - **Pericardial effusion**

**3. Chest X-Ray:**
- **Cardiomegaly**: Cardiothoracic ratio >50%
- **Pulmonary edema**:
  - Cephalization (↑ vascular markings in upper lung fields)
  - Kerley B lines (interstitial edema)
  - Pleural effusions (bilateral)
  - "Bat-wing" or "butterfly" pattern (alveolar edema)

**4. Electrocardiogram (ECG):**
- Often abnormal in HF (normal ECG makes HF unlikely)
- **Findings**:
  - LV hypertrophy
  - Q waves (prior MI)
  - Atrial fibrillation
  - Bundle branch blocks (especially LBBB in HFrEF)
  - Low voltage (amyloidosis, pericardial effusion)

**5. Laboratory Tests:**
- **CBC**: Anemia (worsens HF)
- **Comprehensive metabolic panel**:
  - **Renal function** (creatinine, eGFR): Cardiorenal syndrome
  - **Electrolytes**: Hyponatremia (dilutional), hyperkalemia (RAAS inhibitors), hypokalemia (diuretics)
- **Thyroid function (TSH)**: Hyperthyroidism or hypothyroidism can cause/worsen HF
- **Lipid panel**: Assess CAD risk
- **Troponin**: If acute coronary syndrome suspected
- **Iron studies**: Iron deficiency worsens HF symptoms

**6. Additional Testing (Selected Patients):**
- **Coronary angiography**: If CAD suspected (most common cause of HFrEF)
- **Cardiac MRI**: Assess myocardial viability, infiltrative disease, myocarditis
- **Cardiopulmonary exercise testing**: Assess functional capacity, transplant evaluation
- **Right heart catheterization**: Hemodynamic assessment if diagnosis uncertain, transplant evaluation

### Treatment

**Goals:**
1. **Relieve symptoms** (improve quality of life)
2. **Prevent hospitalization**
3. **Slow disease progression**
4. **Improve survival**

#### Treatment of HFrEF (Systolic Dysfunction)

**Proven Mortality Benefit** - Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy (GDMT)

**1. ACE Inhibitors (ACE-I) or Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs):**
- **First-line therapy** for all HFrEF patients
- **ACE-I**: Enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril
- **ARBs** (if ACE-I intolerant): Losartan, valsartan, candesartan
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Block RAAS → ↓ angiotensin II and aldosterone
  - ↓ Afterload (vasodilation)
  - ↓ Preload (↓ volume retention)
  - **Prevent/reverse ventricular remodeling**
  - ↓ Sympathetic activation
- **Benefits**: ↓ Mortality 20-30%, ↓ hospitalization, improve symptoms
- **Titrate to target doses** (proven in trials)
- **Side effects**: Hyperkalemia, ↑ creatinine, cough (ACE-I), angioedema (rare)
- **Monitor**: K⁺, creatinine at 1-2 weeks

**2. Beta-Blockers:**
- **Carvedilol, metoprolol succinate (Toprol-XL), bisoprolol**
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Block sympathetic activation
  - ↓ Heart rate → ↑ diastolic filling time, ↓ O₂ demand
  - ↓ Arrhythmias
  - **Prevent/reverse ventricular remodeling**
- **Benefits**: ↓ Mortality 30-35%, ↓ sudden cardiac death, improve symptoms
- **Start low, go slow**: Begin at low dose (patient may feel worse initially), titrate up over weeks-months
- **Do NOT stop abruptly**: Risk of rebound
- **Side effects**: Bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, worsening HF initially (transient)

**3. Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs) - Aldosterone Antagonists:**
- **Spironolactone, eplerenone**
- **Indications**: HFrEF with NYHA class II-IV symptoms (despite ACE-I + BB) OR post-MI with EF ≤40%
- **Mechanism**: Block aldosterone → ↓ Na⁺ retention, ↓ K⁺ excretion, ↓ myocardial fibrosis
- **Benefits**: ↓ Mortality 30%, ↓ hospitalization
- **Side effects**: **Hyperkalemia** (monitor closely), gynecomastia (spironolactone)
- **Monitor**: K⁺, creatinine closely
- **Contraindicated**: If K⁺ >5.0 or creatinine >2.5

**4. SGLT2 Inhibitors (Newest Addition to GDMT):**
- **Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin**
- Originally diabetes drugs; now proven benefit in HF (even non-diabetics)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - Inhibit sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 in kidney
  - ↑ Glucose and Na⁺ excretion → osmotic diuresis
  - Cardioprotective mechanisms (not fully understood): ↓ inflammation, ↓ fibrosis, metabolic effects
- **Benefits**: ↓ HF hospitalization 30%, ↓ cardiovascular death, improve symptoms and quality of life
- **Landmark trials**: DAPA-HF, EMPEROR-Reduced
- **Recommended for ALL HFrEF** patients (regardless of diabetes status)
- **Side effects**: Genital mycotic infections, euglycemic DKA (rare)

**5. Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitor (ARNI):**
- **Sacubitril/valsartan (Entresto)**
- **Combination**: ARB (valsartan) + neprilysin inhibitor (sacubitril)
- **Neprilysin inhibition**: ↑ Natriuretic peptides (ANP, BNP) → vasodilation, natriuresis
- **Superior to ACE-I alone**: ↓ Mortality 20% compared to enalapril
- **Indication**: Replace ACE-I/ARB in HFrEF patients with persistent symptoms
- **Side effects**: Hypotension, angioedema (rare but serious)
- **36-hour washout** required after ACE-I before starting (risk of angioedema)

**6. Diuretics (Symptom Relief - No Mortality Benefit):**
- **Loop diuretics**: Furosemide (Lasix), bumetanide, torsemide
- **Indication**: Volume overload, congestion
- **Mechanism**: ↑ Na⁺ and H₂O excretion
- **Benefits**: Rapid symptom relief (dyspnea, edema)
- **Titrate to euvolemia** (dry weight)
- **Daily weights**: ↑ 2-3 lbs in days → ↑ diuretic dose
- **Side effects**: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, ↑ creatinine, hyperuricemia
- **Monitor**: Daily weights, electrolytes

**Optimal HFrEF Medical Regimen ("Fantastic Four"):**
1. ACE-I/ARB/ARNI
2. Beta-blocker
3. MRA (aldosterone antagonist)
4. SGLT2 inhibitor

**All four together** → ↓ Mortality ~50-60% compared to no treatment

**Additional Therapies:**

**7. Hydralazine + Isosorbide Dinitrate:**
- **Indication**: African Americans with HFrEF (↓ mortality in this population); alternative if ACE-I/ARB contraindicated
- **Mechanism**: Vasodilation (↓ afterload and preload)

**8. Ivabradine:**
- **Indication**: HFrEF with sinus rhythm, HR ≥70 despite beta-blocker
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits If current in SA node → ↓ heart rate (no effect on BP or contractility)
- **Benefits**: ↓ HF hospitalization

**9. Digoxin:**
- **Indication**: HFrEF with atrial fibrillation (rate control); persistent symptoms despite GDMT
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → ↑ contractility
- **Benefits**: Improves symptoms, ↓ hospitalization; **NO mortality benefit**
- **Narrow therapeutic window**: Monitor digoxin levels (goal 0.5-0.9 ng/mL)
- **Toxicity**: Arrhythmias, GI symptoms, visual changes

#### Device Therapy

**Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD):**
- **Indication**: HFrEF with EF ≤35% (primary prevention of sudden cardiac death)
- Detects and terminates ventricular tachycardia/fibrillation
- ↓ Sudden cardiac death by 50-60%

**Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy (CRT) - Biventricular Pacemaker:**
- **Indication**: HFrEF with EF ≤35%, NYHA class II-IV, **wide QRS ≥150 ms** (especially LBBB)
- Paces both ventricles simultaneously → restore synchronous contraction
- **Benefits**: Improve symptoms, ↓ hospitalization, ↓ mortality

#### Advanced Therapies (Stage D - Refractory HF)

**Left Ventricular Assist Device (LVAD):**
- Mechanical pump to support failing LV
- **Bridge to transplant** or **destination therapy** (permanent)

**Heart Transplantation:**
- Definitive treatment for end-stage HF
- Limited by donor availability

#### Treatment of HFpEF (Diastolic Dysfunction)

**Challenge:** **No therapies proven to reduce mortality** in HFpEF (unlike HFrEF)

**Goals:** Symptom management, treat underlying causes

**1. Diuretics:**
- **Cornerstone of symptom management**
- Relieve pulmonary congestion
- **Use cautiously**: Excessive diuresis → ↓ preload → ↓ CO (HFpEF relies on adequate filling)

**2. SGLT2 Inhibitors:**
- **Empagliflozin** showed benefit in HFpEF (EMPEROR-Preserved trial)
- ↓ HF hospitalization
- **Now recommended** for HFpEF

**3. Blood Pressure Control:**
- **Treat hypertension aggressively** (most common cause)
- ACE-I/ARBs, CCBs, beta-blockers
- Goal BP <130/80 mmHg

**4. Heart Rate Control:**
- If atrial fibrillation: Rate control with beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine CCBs
- Longer diastolic filling time improves symptoms

**5. Treat Underlying Conditions:**
- **Atrial fibrillation**: Rhythm or rate control
- **Obesity**: Weight loss improves symptoms
- **Diabetes**: Glycemic control
- **Sleep apnea**: CPAP
- **Coronary disease**: Revascularization if ischemia
- **Valvular disease**: Surgical correction if indicated

**6. Avoid:**
- Excessive diuresis (need adequate preload)
- Tachycardia (shortens diastolic filling)

### Herbal Therapeutic Targets in Heart Failure

**CRITICAL:** Heart failure requires intensive medical management with proven therapies (ACE-I, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2-I). Herbs are **adjunctive only** and must be coordinated with cardiologist. **Never replace GDMT with herbs.**

| **Positive Inotrope** | ↑ Contractility | Hawthorn (mild), Digitalis (pharmaceutical, not herbal use) |
| **Vasodilation** | ↓ Afterload | Hawthorn, Hibiscus, Garlic |
| **Diuretic** | ↓ Volume overload | Dandelion leaf, Hibiscus, Parsley |
| **Cardioprotective** | Support myocardial metabolism, ↓ remodeling | Hawthorn, CoQ10, L-carnitine, Taurine |
| **Antioxidant** | ↓ Oxidative stress | Hawthorn, Green tea, Turmeric |
| **Anti-Arrhythmic** | Stabilize rhythm | Hawthorn, Magnesium, Fish oil |
| **Anti-Inflammatory** | ↓ Myocardial inflammation | Turmeric, Fish oil |

### Evidence-Based Herbal Support for HF

**1. Hawthorn (Crataegus spp.):**
- **Most evidence** for herbal therapy in HF
- **Dose**: 900-1800 mg standardized extract daily (divided doses)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - **Positive inotrope** (↑ contractility without ↑ O₂ demand) - via ↑ calcium sensitivity, phosphodiesterase inhibition
  - Vasodilation (↓ afterload)
  - ↑ Coronary blood flow
  - Antioxidant
  - Anti-arrhythmic
- **Evidence**:
  - **SPICE trial** (2008): 2681 patients with HFrEF (NYHA II-III) randomized to hawthorn extract vs placebo
    - **No reduction in mortality or hospitalization** (primary endpoint)
    - **Improved symptoms** in subset analysis
  - **Meta-analyses**: Improvement in exercise tolerance, symptoms (dyspnea, fatigue), quality of life
  - **Grade B evidence** for NYHA class II-III HF as adjunctive therapy
- **Safety**: Excellent; well-tolerated; no significant drug interactions with cardiac medications
- **Clinical use**: **Adjunctive to GDMT** for symptom improvement; NOT a replacement

**2. Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10):**
- **Dose**: 100-300 mg daily (ubiquinol form preferred)
- **Mechanisms**:
  - **Essential cofactor** in mitochondrial ATP production (myocardial energy)
  - Antioxidant
  - **Deficiency common** in HF patients (and worsened by statins)
- **Evidence**:
  - **Q-SYMBIO trial** (2014): 420 patients with chronic HFrEF randomized to CoQ10 100 mg TID vs placebo
    - **↓ Cardiovascular mortality** by 43%
    - **↓ HF hospitalizations**
    - Improved NYHA class
  - **Grade B evidence** for adjunctive use in HF
- **Safety**: Excellent
- **Clinical pearl**: Especially important if patient on statin

**3. L-Carnitine:**
- **Dose**: 1-3g daily
- **Mechanism**: Facilitates fatty acid transport into mitochondria → ↑ myocardial energy
- **Evidence**: Some studies show improved exercise capacity, symptoms; mixed results
- **Safety**: Well-tolerated

**4. Taurine:**
- **Dose**: 2-6g daily
- **Mechanism**: Amino acid; cardioprotective, ↑ contractility, Ca²⁺ regulation, antioxidant
- **Evidence**: Small studies show benefit in HF; limited data
- **Safety**: Well-tolerated

**5. Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA):**
- **Dose**: 1-4g daily
- **Mechanisms**: Anti-inflammatory, anti-arrhythmic, modest hemodynamic benefit
- **Evidence**:
  - **GISSI-HF trial**: ↓ Cardiovascular mortality and HF hospitalization (modest benefit)
- **Safety**: Excellent; safe with cardiac medications

**6. Magnesium:**
- **Dose**: 300-400 mg daily (if not contraindicated)
- **Mechanism**: Anti-arrhythmic, vasodilation, cofactor in ATP production
- **Deficiency common**: Especially with loop diuretics
- **Repletion important** for optimal cardiac function

**7. Thiamine (Vitamin B1):**
- **Dose**: 100-300 mg daily
- **Indication**: HF patients on chronic loop diuretics (thiamine depletion), alcoholics
- **Deficiency → "wet beriberi"** (high-output HF)
- **Repletion improves EF** in deficient patients

### Herbs to AVOID in Heart Failure

**CONTRAINDICATED:**
- **Licorice (Glycyrrhiza glabra)**: Mineralocorticoid effects → Na⁺ retention, K⁺ loss, ↑ BP, edema - WORSENS HF
- **Ephedra (Ma Huang)**: Sympathomimetic → ↑ HR, ↑ BP, arrhythmogenic - DANGEROUS
- **Yohimbe**: Sympathomimetic, ↑ BP
- **Ginseng (high doses)**: May ↑ BP, interact with warfarin (if patient on anticoagulation)

**Use Cautiously:**
- **Garlic**: Antiplatelet effects (monitor if on anticoagulation)
- **Ginkgo**: Antiplatelet effects
- **St. John's Wort**: CYP3A4 inducer → ↓ levels of many cardiac meds (digoxin, warfarin, statins)

### Comprehensive HF Support Protocol

**For HFrEF (Adjunctive to GDMT):**
- **Continue all prescribed medications** (ACE-I/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2-I, diuretics)
- **Hawthorn**: 900-1800 mg daily (with cardiology approval)
- **CoQ10**: 200-300 mg daily (especially if on statin)
- **Omega-3**: 2-4g daily
- **Magnesium**: 400 mg daily (if not contraindicated, monitor levels)
- **Thiamine**: 100 mg daily (if on chronic loop diuretics)
- Low-sodium diet (<2g/day)
- Fluid restriction (1.5-2L/day if NYHA III-IV)
- Daily weights (notify MD if ↑ 2-3 lbs in days)
- Cardiac rehabilitation

### Lifestyle Management

**Sodium Restriction:**
- **Goal**: <2000 mg (2g) sodium/day
- Severe HF: <1500 mg/day
- Read labels, avoid processed foods, canned soups, deli meats

**Fluid Restriction:**
- **NYHA III-IV**: <1.5-2 liters/day total fluid (including food)

**Daily Weights:**
- Weigh same time daily (morning, after urination, before eating)
- **↑ 2-3 lbs in 1-2 days or ↑ 5 lbs in week** → call doctor (fluid retention)

**Exercise:**
- **Cardiac rehabilitation**: Supervised exercise program
- ↑ Exercise tolerance, ↑ quality of life, ↓ hospitalizations
- **Avoid in acute decompensation**; start when stable

**Avoid:**
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, naproxen): Cause Na⁺ retention, worsen HF, ↓ efficacy of ACE-I/diuretics
- **Alcohol**: Toxic to myocardium; max 1 drink/day or abstain if alcoholic cardiomyopathy
- **Smoking**: Cessation essential

**Immunizations:**
- Influenza vaccine (annual)
- Pneumococcal vaccine
- COVID-19 vaccine

### Prognosis

**Variable:**
- **5-year mortality**: ~50% overall (worse than many cancers)
- **NYHA class IV**: 1-year mortality 30-40%
- **Improved with GDMT**: Modern therapy significantly improves survival

**Poor Prognostic Factors:**
- Very low EF (<20%)
- Advanced NYHA class (III-IV)
- Elevated BNP
- Renal dysfunction
- Hyponatremia
- Cachexia (muscle wasting)
- Frequent hospitalizations

**Clinical Note:** Heart failure is chronic, progressive, and requires lifelong management. **Guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT)** is essential and saves lives - ACE-I/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2-I are all proven to reduce mortality. Hawthorn and CoQ10 have best evidence for adjunctive herbal support but never replace GDMT. Emphasize sodium restriction, daily weights, medication adherence. HFpEF is challenging - no mortality benefit from most therapies, focus on symptom management and treating underlying conditions. Patient education crucial - recognize decompensation signs early.
