# Pathophysiology Reference: Renal Conditions

**Context:** This monograph provides an advanced pathophysiological analysis of kidney disorders. It focuses on the molecular mechanisms of glomerular injury, tubular necrosis, and progressive renal fibrosis.

---

## Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

### Etiology & Classification
Abrupt decline in renal function (hours to days) leading to retention of nitrogenous waste products.
*   **Prerenal (60-70%):** Hypoperfusion (Hypovolemia, Heart Failure, Sepsis).
*   **Intrinsic (25-40%):** Direct parenchymal damage.
    *   **Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN):** Ischemic or Toxic (Contrast, Aminoglycosides).
    *   **Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN):** Drug hypersensitivity (NSAIDs, Penicillins).
    *   **Glomerulonephritis:** Immune-mediated.
*   **Postrenal (5-10%):** Obstruction (BPH, Stones, Tumors).

### Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis (ATN)
**Ischemic/Toxic Injury to Tubular Epithelium:**
1.  **Initiation:** Hypoxia/Toxin $\rightarrow$ ATP depletion $\rightarrow$ loss of cell polarity (Na+/K+ ATPase redistribution).
2.  **Extension:** Cell death (necrosis/apoptosis) $\rightarrow$ detachment from basement membrane.
3.  **Maintenance:**
    *   **Cast Formation:** Dead cells + Tamm-Horsfall protein $\rightarrow$ tubular obstruction.
    *   **Backleak:** Filtrate leaks back into interstitium $\rightarrow$ edema.
    *   **Vasoconstriction:** Tubuloglomerular feedback (high NaCl at macula densa) $\rightarrow$ afferent arteriole constriction $\rightarrow$ $\downarrow$ GFR.
4.  **Recovery:** Regeneration of tubular cells (if basement membrane intact).

### Systemic Pathophysiology
*   **Azotemia:** Accumulation of Urea (BUN) and Creatinine.
*   **Fluid/Electrolyte Imbalance:** Hyperkalemia, Metabolic Acidosis (AGMA), Fluid Overload.
*   **Uremia:** Encephalopathy, Pericarditis, Platelet dysfunction.

### Pathophysiological Cascade
**[Flow: Tubular Injury & GFR Decline]**
1.  **Initial Insult:** **Ischemia/Toxin** $\rightarrow$ Tubular epithelial cell necrosis.
2.  **Compensatory Response:** **Tubuloglomerular Feedback**. Macula densa senses high NaCl (due to failed reabsorption upstream) $\rightarrow$ releases Adenosine $\rightarrow$ constricts afferent arteriole.
3.  **Maladaptive Consequence:**
    *   **GFR Drop:** Vasoconstriction reduces glomerular perfusion pressure to "rest" the injured tubule.
    *   **Fluid Retention:** Oliguria leads to volume overload.
4.  **Symptom Mechanism:**
    *   **Azotemia (Nausea/Confusion):** Urea accumulates in blood $\rightarrow$ crosses BBB $\rightarrow$ neurotoxicity.
    *   **Acidosis (Kussmaul Breathing):** Kidney fails to excrete H+ $\rightarrow$ metabolic acidosis $\rightarrow$ respiratory compensation.

### Clinical Correlations
*   **Oliguria:** Urine output < 400 mL/day (common in maintenance phase).
*   **Polyuria:** During recovery phase (diuretic phase).

### Diagnostic Biomarkers
*   **BUN/Creatinine Ratio:** >20:1 (Prerenal), <15:1 (Intrinsic).
*   **FeNa (Fractional Excretion of Sodium):** <1% (Prerenal - avid Na+ reabsorption), >2% (ATN - tubular damage).
*   **Urinalysis:** Muddy brown casts (ATN), WBC casts (AIN), RBC casts (Glomerulonephritis).

## Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

### Etiology
Progressive, irreversible loss of renal function > 3 months.
*   **Top Causes:** Diabetes Mellitus (Diabetic Nephropathy), Hypertension (Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis), Glomerulonephritis.

### Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis
**Final Common Pathway: Renal Fibrosis:**
1.  **Glomerular Hyperfiltration:** Loss of nephrons $\rightarrow$ compensatory hypertrophy of remaining nephrons $\rightarrow$ increased intraglomerular pressure.
2.  **Podocyte Injury:** Mechanical stress $\rightarrow$ podocyte loss $\rightarrow$ proteinuria.
3.  **Mesangial Expansion:** TGF-$\beta$ upregulation $\rightarrow$ increased matrix production.
4.  **Tubulointerstitial Fibrosis:** Proteinuria is toxic to tubules $\rightarrow$ inflammation $\rightarrow$ fibroblasts lay down collagen $\rightarrow$ scarring.

### Systemic Pathophysiology
*   **Mineral & Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD):**
    *   $\downarrow$ Phosphate excretion $\rightarrow$ Hyperphosphatemia.
    *   $\downarrow$ 1$\alpha$-hydroxylase $\rightarrow$ $\downarrow$ Active Vitamin D (Calcitriol) $\rightarrow$ Hypocalcemia.
    *   **Secondary Hyperparathyroidism:** $\uparrow$ PTH to correct Ca/PO4 $\rightarrow$ Renal Osteodystrophy (bone resorption).
*   **Anemia:** $\downarrow$ Erythropoietin (EPO) production by peritubular interstitial cells.
*   **Cardiovascular:** Accelerated atherosclerosis, calcification.

### Pathophysiological Cascade
**[Flow: Nephron Loss & Systemic Toxicity]**
1.  **Initial Insult:** **Nephron Loss** (Diabetes/HTN).
2.  **Compensatory Response:** **Hyperfiltration**. Remaining nephrons work harder (hypertrophy) to maintain GFR.
3.  **Maladaptive Consequence:**
    *   **Glomerulosclerosis:** High pressure damages the delicate filter $\rightarrow$ more nephrons die (vicious cycle).
    *   **Uremia:** Loss of excretory function $\rightarrow$ accumulation of "middle molecules" (toxins).
4.  **Symptom Mechanism:**
    *   **Bone Pain (Renal Osteodystrophy):** High Phosphate binds Calcium $\rightarrow$ Low Ca2+ triggers PTH $\rightarrow$ Osteoclasts strip calcium from bone.
    *   **Anemia (Fatigue):** Fibrosis destroys EPO-producing cells $\rightarrow$ bone marrow stops making RBCs.

### Clinical Correlations
*   **Stages:** 1 (GFR >90) to 5 (GFR <15 - End Stage Renal Disease).
*   **Uremic Frost:** Urea crystals on skin (rare/late).

### Diagnostic Biomarkers
*   **eGFR:** Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate.
*   **Albuminuria:** Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR). Early marker of diabetic nephropathy.

## Nephrotic Syndrome

### Etiology
Glomerular disease characterized by massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day).
*   **Causes:** Minimal Change Disease (Children), Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), Membranous Nephropathy, Diabetic Nephropathy, Amyloidosis.

### Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis
**Podocytopathy (Loss of Charge/Size Barrier):**
1.  **Podocyte Effacement:** Fusion of foot processes.
2.  **Loss of Negative Charge:** Loss of heparan sulfate in GBM (Minimal Change Disease) allows albumin (negatively charged) to pass.
3.  **Immune Deposition:** Subepithelial deposits (Membranous) damage podocytes via Membrane Attack Complex (C5b-9).

### Systemic Pathophysiology
*   **Proteinuria:** Loss of Albumin, Antithrombin III, Immunoglobulins.
*   **Hypoalbuminemia:** $\downarrow$ Oncotic pressure $\rightarrow$ Edema.
*   **Edema:** Generalized (Anasarca), Periorbital.
*   **Hyperlipidemia:** Liver ramps up lipoprotein synthesis in response to low oncotic pressure.
*   **Hypercoagulability:** Loss of Antithrombin III $\rightarrow$ DVT/PE/Renal Vein Thrombosis.

### Pathophysiological Cascade
**[Flow: Barrier Failure & Edema]**
1.  **Initial Insult:** **Podocyte Injury** (loss of foot processes).
2.  **Compensatory Response:** **Hepatic Protein Synthesis**. Liver ramps up production of Albumin and Lipoproteins to restore oncotic pressure.
3.  **Maladaptive Consequence:**
    *   **Hypoalbuminemia:** Liver cannot keep up with massive urinary loss (>3.5g/day).
    *   **Hyperlipidemia:** Lipoproteins are too large to be lost, so they accumulate.
4.  **Symptom Mechanism:**
    *   **Anasarca (Edema):** Capillary oncotic pressure drops $\rightarrow$ Starling forces favor fluid movement into interstitium.
    *   **Thrombosis:** Antithrombin III (small protein) is lost in urine $\rightarrow$ hypercoagulable state.

### Clinical Correlations
*   **Frothy Urine:** Due to high protein.

### Diagnostic Biomarkers
*   **Urinalysis:** 4+ Protein, Fatty casts ("Maltese Cross" under polarized light).

## Nephritic Syndrome

### Etiology
Glomerular disease characterized by inflammation, hematuria, and varying degrees of proteinuria.
*   **Causes:** Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN), IgA Nephropathy (Berger's), Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN - Lupus, Goodpasture's, Vasculitis).

### Molecular & Cellular Pathogenesis
**Glomerular Inflammation (Proliferative):**
1.  **Immune Complex Deposition:** Subendothelial or Mesangial deposits.
2.  **Inflammation:** Complement activation $\rightarrow$ neutrophil influx $\rightarrow$ glomerular damage.
3.  **GBM Breaks:** RBCs leak into tubule $\rightarrow$ RBC casts.
4.  **Crescent Formation (RPGN):** Fibrin and macrophages in Bowman's space compress glomerular tuft.

### Systemic Pathophysiology
*   **Hematuria:** Dysmorphic RBCs.
*   **Oliguria & Azotemia:** $\downarrow$ GFR due to inflammation.
*   **Hypertension:** Salt and water retention.

### Pathophysiological Cascade
**[Flow: Glomerular Inflammation & Hematuria]**
1.  **Initial Insult:** **Immune Complex Deposition** in Glomerulus.
2.  **Compensatory Response:** **Inflammatory Influx**. Neutrophils arrive to clear complexes, releasing ROS and proteases.
3.  **Maladaptive Consequence:**
    *   **GBM Rupture:** Physical breaks in the basement membrane.
    *   **Crescent Formation:** Fibrin leaks into Bowman's space $\rightarrow$ scar tissue crushes the glomerulus.
4.  **Symptom Mechanism:**
    *   **Hematuria:** RBCs squeeze through GBM gaps $\rightarrow$ become dysmorphic.
    *   **Hypertension:** GFR drops $\rightarrow$ Sodium retention $\rightarrow$ Volume expansion.

### Clinical Correlations
*   **Cola-colored Urine:** Hematuria.
*   **IgA Nephropathy:** Hematuria concurrent with respiratory/GI infection.
*   **PSGN:** Hematuria 2-4 weeks after strep throat.

### Diagnostic Biomarkers
*   **Urinalysis:** RBC Casts (Pathognomonic), Dysmorphic RBCs.
*   **Serology:** ASO titer (Strep), Anti-GBM (Goodpasture's), ANCA (Vasculitis).
