*by Peter Attia, MD, with Bill Gifford*

Longevity in this book means **living longer and living better**: adding healthy, functional years, not just more time on the clock. The core thesis is that the diseases most likely to kill or disable us (heart disease, cancer, neurodegenerative disease, and metabolic disease) develop silently over decades, so we must **act far earlier and more proactively** than traditional medicine recommends. Attia calls this shift “Medicine 3.0”: using data, personalized risk assessment, and long-term frameworks to shape our exercise, nutrition, sleep, and emotional health. This handbook distills those principles into a practical, non-ideological playbook.

### Core Principles

* **Longevity = Lifespan × Healthspan**

  * It’s not enough to live longer; the goal is to maximize **years of good function**—physical, cognitive, and emotional.
  * Attia emphasizes “bending the curve” so that decline is delayed and compressed into the very end of life. 

* **The Four Horsemen drive slow death**

  * Most people in developed countries now die from **chronic, slow diseases**:

    * Atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease
    * Cancer
    * Neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., Alzheimer’s)
    * Metabolic disease/type 2 diabetes and related dysfunction
  * These conditions develop over decades; by the time they’re diagnosed, damage is often extensive. 

* **Modern medicine is great at fast death, poor at slow death**

  * Emergency and acute care (trauma, infections, critical surgeries) is highly effective.
  * For chronic diseases, the system mainly reacts late, manages symptoms, and buys a little time instead of preventing disease in the first place. 

* **Medicine 3.0: proactive, preventive, personalized**

  * Shift from “catching falling eggs” (treating late disease) to “stopping the egg thrower” (preventing or delaying disease onset).
  * Use long-term risk modeling, early metrics, and personalized interventions instead of one-size-fits-all guidelines. 

* **Think in decades, not weeks**

  * Health decisions should be guided by what they do to your **trajectory over 10–40 years**, not short-term weight loss or lab blips.
  * Early action has compounding benefits; waiting until diagnosis wastes the most valuable window for intervention. 

* **Movement, nutrition, sleep, and emotional health are the four levers**

  * **Exercise** is the most powerful “longevity drug,” with broad impact on lifespan, healthspan, and cognitive function. 
  * **Nutrition** should be built on biochemistry and individual response, not ideology.
  * **Sleep** is a non-negotiable repair window for brain and body.
  * **Emotional health**—trauma, relationships, stress—can quietly undermine every other domain if ignored.

* **No single prescription fits everyone**

  * There is no universal “best diet,” exercise plan, or supplement stack. The right plan depends on your **goals, risks, history, and constraints**.
  * You need a framework to test and adjust tactics rather than blindly follow a fixed program. 

* **Data-informed, not data-obsessed**

  * Use metrics (labs, imaging, performance tests, sleep data) to guide decisions, but avoid chasing numbers at the expense of overall wellbeing.

* **Emotional health is foundational, not optional**

  * Chronic emotional stress, unresolved trauma, and dysfunctional relationships can drive harmful behaviors and physiological stress.
  * Longevity work fails if your inner life is neglected.

### Practical Playbook

#### Longevity Mindset & Medicine 3.0

**What to do**

* Adopt the goal: **live longer while maintaining high function as late as possible**.
* Accept that **chronic diseases start much earlier** than diagnosis; act as if you are already at risk.
* Define your **“Marginal Decade”**—the last 10 years you’re likely to live—and work backward from how you want to function then.
* Embrace **probabilities and trade-offs**: no intervention guarantees an outcome, but you can stack the odds in your favor.

**How to do it**

* Map your **family history and existing risk factors** (e.g., strong family history of heart disease, dementia, or diabetes).
* Identify your most likely **“proximate Horseman”** (the disease most likely to get you) and prioritize tactics that target it. 
* Use the **objective–strategy–tactics** framework (detailed later) to structure all interventions.
* Engage with clinicians who are comfortable with **pre-emptive testing and risk reduction**, not just guideline-driven reactive care.

**Why it matters**

* Most of the harm from chronic disease is determined **long before diagnosis**, when you still feel fine. Acting early captures the biggest gains in lifespan and healthspan.

#### Movement & Exercise

Attia treats exercise as the single most powerful, evidence-backed longevity intervention.

##### What to do

* Aim to build and maintain:

  * **Strength** (ability to generate force)
  * **Stability** (control of movement, balance, joint integrity)
  * **Aerobic efficiency** (sub-maximal/cardio fitness)
  * **Peak aerobic capacity** (VO₂ max / ability to go “all out”) 
* Move from being “barely getting by” to being **robustly capable** in all four domains.
* Train year-round with a **periodized mix** of strength, stability, and aerobic work.

##### How to do it (structure & emphasis)

* **Baseline movement**

  * Minimize prolonged sitting; incorporate walking and low-level movement throughout the day.
  * Use stairs, walking meetings, and short movement breaks as “default” behaviors.

* **Strength training**

  * Train major muscle groups **at least 2–3 times per week**.
  * Prioritize movements that support late-life independence:

    * Squat variations (sitting/standing from a chair, toilets, cars)
    * Hinge (picking up objects safely)
    * Push and pull (opening doors, carrying groceries)
    * Loaded carries (carrying children, luggage).
  * Use **progressive overload**: gradually increase resistance, volume, or complexity.

* **Stability & functional patterns**

  * Include **balance, coordination, and control work** every week, especially as you age.
  * Practice getting down to the floor and up again:

    * Incorporate exercises like the **“Barry Get-Up”**—a choreographed sequence to rise from the floor or a low seat—which is both a diagnostic test and a training tool for independence. 
  * Train single-leg balance, rotational control, and trunk stability to reduce falls and injuries.

* **Aerobic training**

  * Include **low-to-moderate intensity “steady-state”** work that you can sustain while talking (often described as “easy but not trivial”).
  * Add **high-intensity intervals** to challenge peak capacity, with adequate recovery and good technique.
  * Rotate modalities (walking, cycling, rowing, swimming, etc.) to reduce overuse injuries and keep adherence high.

* **Testing & feedback**

  * Track functional benchmarks that matter for your Centenarian Decathlon goals (see Frameworks):

    * Time to walk a set distance
    * Ability to carry a given load
    * Ability to get off the floor unaided
    * Stairs daily without undue effort

##### Why it matters

* Exercise reduces mortality and delays disability **more than any single drug or intervention**. 
* It protects against the Four Horsemen by improving metabolic health, vascular health, and brain function, and likely by direct effects on neurobiology.
* It increases **reserve capacity** so life’s inevitable hits (illness, accidents) are less likely to push you into permanent decline.

#### Strength & Stability

**What to do**

* Treat **muscle mass and strength as critical organ systems**, especially after midlife.
* Build and maintain strength in **hips, legs, trunk, and grip**, which are strongly linked to independence and fall risk.
* Train **stability and balance** deliberately, not as an afterthought.

**How to do it**

* Use resistance (bodyweight, bands, machines, free weights) for:

  * Lower body: squats, lunges, hip hinges, step-ups.
  * Upper body: pushups/presses, rows, pull-downs/pull-ups.
  * Core: anti-rotation, anti-flexion/extension, carries.
* Practice **transitional movements**: getting off the floor, getting in/out of a car, stepping over obstacles.
* Implement micro-progressions (slightly deeper, slower, heavier, or more controlled over time).
* Add **balance drills**: single-leg stands, heel-to-toe walking, unstable surface work (when safe and supervised if needed).

**Why it matters**

* Decline in strength and power is a major contributor to **frailty, falls, fractures, and loss of independence**.
* Stability work reduces injury risk and ensures that cardiovascular and strength gains are usable in real life. 

---

#### Aerobic Fitness & Conditioning

**What to do**

* Build a strong **aerobic base** and maintain or improve **peak aerobic capacity** across the lifespan.
* Use a mix of **steady-state “easy” work** and **short, intense efforts**.

**How to do it**

* Choose low-impact modes you can sustain (walking, cycling, rowing, swimming).
* Structure sessions to:

  * Spend significant time at a comfortable, steady effort where breathing is elevated but conversation is still possible.
  * Include periodic higher-intensity intervals where speaking in full sentences is difficult, separated by full recovery.
* Progress duration, frequency, or intensity gradually to avoid overuse injuries and burnout.

**Why it matters**

* Aerobic fitness is a strong predictor of **all-cause mortality and late-life function**.
* Better aerobic capacity supports **brain health, metabolic control, and resilience** to illness and surgery.

#### Nutrition & Metabolic Health

Attia emphasizes **nutritional biochemistry and individual response** over diet ideology.

##### What to do

* Focus first on **energy balance** (how much you eat), then on **macronutrient composition**, especially **adequate protein**.
* Prioritize **whole, minimally processed foods**; limit ultra-processed, hyper-palatable foods that bypass normal satiety.
* Avoid **sugar-sweetened and fructose-sweetened beverages** (including juices) as a routine intake. 
* Use diet to target your **dominant risk**: metabolic disease, cardiovascular risk, or others.

##### How to do it

* **Three levers of nutrition** (expanded in Frameworks):

  1. **Calorie restriction** – total energy intake across days/weeks.
  2. **Dietary restriction** – which macronutrients or food types you emphasize or limit.
  3. **Time restriction** – when you eat (meal timing, feeding window). 
* Choose patterns you can **sustain long term**:

  * Some people respond better to lower carbohydrate intake, especially with insulin resistance.
  * Others do well with different macronutrient balances so long as protein needs are met and calories are appropriate.
* Prioritize **protein intake**, especially as you age, to support:

  * Muscle maintenance and growth
  * Satiety and metabolic health
* Manage **carbohydrate quality and quantity**:

  * Favor lower-glycemic, fiber-rich sources (vegetables, legumes, whole grains as tolerated).
  * Limit refined starches and sugars, especially in liquid form.
* Tailor **fat intake** according to your lipoprotein and cardiovascular risk profile:

  * Monitor how saturated fats and total fat intake affect your apoB and triglycerides if you have access to advanced testing. 

##### Why it matters

* Metabolic dysfunction is central to **type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and likely neurodegeneration**.
* Nutrition is a powerful lever for improving **glucose control, insulin sensitivity, lipids, and body composition**.
* The best diet is the one that:

  * Improves your **key metabolic markers**
  * Supports **muscle and performance**
  * Fits your **psychology and lifestyle** enough to sustain.

#### Sleep & Circadian Health

**What to do**

* Treat sleep as a **core pillar of performance and longevity**, not a negotiable luxury.
* Aim for **consistent, adequate sleep duration** (typically ~7–9 hours/night for most adults, adjusted individually).
* Address insomnia and poor sleep proactively; don’t normalize chronic sleep deprivation.

**How to do it**

* **Sleep hygiene basics**:

  * Keep **regular bed and wake times**, including weekends when possible.
  * Create a **dark, cool, quiet** sleep environment.
  * Avoid bright screens and intense light exposure close to bedtime.
  * Limit caffeine to earlier in the day and avoid heavy meals and alcohol before bed.
* **If you can’t fall asleep or wake up and can’t return to sleep**:

  * Get out of bed and go to another room.
  * Do something **relaxing and non-productive** (e.g., reading a dull book, light stretching).
  * Avoid doing work or anything that gives your brain a reason to stay awake. 
* If persistent insomnia continues, consider **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)**, which:

  * Targets anxiety and maladaptive beliefs around sleep.
  * Uses methods like **sleep restriction** to rebuild confidence in your ability to sleep. 

**Why it matters**

* Sleep is a **global repair process** for brain and body, affecting:

  * Memory consolidation and cognitive function
  * Metabolic health and insulin sensitivity
  * Cardiovascular and emotional regulation
* Chronic poor sleep increases risk of **insulin resistance, cognitive decline, and mood disorders**.

#### Emotional Health & Relationships

**What to do**

* Take emotional health as seriously as you take labs and training plans.
* Explore and address **unresolved trauma, anger, shame, perfectionism, and relational patterns** that drive self-destructive behaviors.
* Seek **therapy, coaching, or group support** when emotional patterns repeatedly sabotage your health goals.

**How to do it**

* Identify **recurring emotional loops** (e.g., blowing up under stress, withdrawing, compulsive overwork, emotional eating).
* Connect these loops to downstream health impacts (poor sleep, skipped workouts, substance use, isolation).
* Work with a mental health professional who understands:

  * Attachment patterns
  * Family-of-origin dynamics
  * How emotional defenses may have once been adaptive but are now harmful.
* Develop **skills** in:

  * Communication and conflict resolution
  * Boundary setting
  * Self-compassion and realistic self-evaluation

**Why it matters**

* Emotional suffering and dysfunctional relationships can **erode physical health, shorten lifespan, and make “doing the right things” unsustainable**.
* A longevity strategy that ignores emotional health is incomplete; sooner or later it will break down.

#### Screening, Labs & Risk Management

*(High-level summary only; exact test choices and thresholds should be decided with a clinician.)*

**What to do**

* Use **early, more sensitive markers** to detect risk long before disease manifests.
* Focus testing on your **probable Horsemen**:

  * Cardiovascular (lipids, blood pressure, imaging)
  * Metabolic (glucose and insulin dynamics)
  * Cancer (age- and risk-appropriate screening)
  * Neurodegeneration (risk factors, early cognitive changes)

**How to do it**

* Cardiovascular:

  * Track **lipoprotein measures beyond basic cholesterol** where available (e.g., markers related to apoB-containing particles).
  * Monitor blood pressure and other vascular risk factors.
  * Discuss **imaging** (like coronary artery calcium scoring or other modalities) with your clinician based on age and risk.
* Metabolic:

  * Don’t wait for frank diabetes (e.g., HbA1c ≥6.5%) before acting; treat **prediabetes and “high-normal” values** as serious early signals. 
  * Consider testing that reveals **how you handle glucose over time**, not just a single fasting value.
* Cancer:

  * Follow evidence-based screening for cancers with effective early detection in your age/sex/risk group.
  * Emphasize **risk-reducing behaviors** (e.g., not smoking) that have larger impact than late-stage therapies. 
* Neurodegenerative risk:

  * Address **modifiable drivers**—sleep, vascular health, metabolic health, physical activity—early and aggressively.

**Why it matters**

* Many current guidelines define disease at arbitrary thresholds; **risk accumulates across a continuous spectrum** long before the threshold.
* Starting interventions earlier can prevent or significantly delay disease instead of merely managing late-stage damage.

#### Environment & Daily Life Design

**What to do**

* Design your environment so that **good choices are the default** and unhealthy options require more effort.
* Reduce **friction for exercise, sleep, and healthy eating**, and increase friction for behaviors you want less of.

**How to do it**

* Place exercise equipment where you see it and can use it easily.
* Keep most ultra-processed foods **out of the house**; stock defaults that fit your nutrition plan.
* Align **social life** with people who support or share your health goals.
* Arrange **light exposure** (bright light in the morning, dimmer in the evening) to support circadian rhythms.

**Why it matters**

* Willpower alone is unreliable across decades. **Environment and habit design** make the right behaviors automatic and sustainable.

### Protocols & Checklists

#### Longevity Planning Protocol

1. **Clarify Objectives**

   * Write a short description of how you want to **live in your Marginal Decade** (physical ability, cognitive function, relationships).
2. **List Your Risks**

   * Note family history of heart disease, cancer, dementia, diabetes, or early deaths.
   * Include personal risk factors (smoking history, high blood pressure, obesity, etc.).
3. **Choose Your Primary Horseman**

   * Based on history and early data, identify your **most likely threat** and give it priority in planning. 
4. **Define Strategy**

   * For each domain (movement, nutrition, sleep, emotional health), write 2–3 **strategic aims** (e.g., “build enough leg strength to get off the floor easily at 80”).
5. **Pick Initial Tactics**

   * Select a small number of **doable habits** in each domain (e.g., 2 strength sessions/week, no sugary drinks, fixed bedtime).
6. **Plan Review Cycles**

   * Reassess every **3–6 months**:

     * Are you moving toward your functional goals?
     * Are labs and performance trending better?
     * What needs to be adjusted?

#### Weekly Training Template

*(Adjust volume and intensity to your fitness and medical status.)*

* **Strength (2–3 sessions/week)**

  * Day 1: Lower body + core (e.g., squats, hinges, carries, anti-rotation work).
  * Day 2: Upper body + core (push/pull, overhead where appropriate, carries).
  * Optional Day 3: Mixed total-body strength or emphasis on weak links.

* **Stability & Balance (most days, short sessions)**

  * 5–10 minutes of:

    * Single-leg balance
    * “Barry Get-Up” or similar get-up drills
    * Trunk stability work. 

* **Aerobic Base (2–4 sessions/week)**

  * 30–60 minutes each, easy conversational pace.

* **High-Intensity Work (1–2 sessions/week)**

  * Short intervals of harder effort with full recovery; avoid if injured or contraindicated and progress gradually.

* **Daily Movement**

  * Steps, standing, micro-bouts of movement to avoid long sedentary stretches.

#### Nutrition Implementation Checklist

* □ I know my **primary nutrition goal** (fat loss, muscle gain, glucose control, lipids, or maintenance).
* □ I’ve chosen a **calorie approach** (tracking, structured plan, or constrained food environment).
* □ I’m meeting **protein needs**, especially on training days and as I age. 
* □ My plan defines **which foods I limit or avoid** (e.g., sugary drinks, ultra-processed snacks, frequent desserts). 
* □ I’ve decided how to use **time restriction** (eating window, meal regularity) without compromising total intake or sleep.
* □ I monitor **weight, waist, and key labs** at reasonable intervals and adjust based on trends, not day-to-day noise.
* □ My diet is **sustainable**—I can imagine doing a version of it in 5–10 years.

---

#### Sleep Hygiene Protocol

1. **Anchor your wake time**

   * Choose a realistic wake time and keep it consistent daily.
2. **Set a sleep window**

   * Count back the number of hours you need; choose a target bedtime aligned with your **chronotype** when possible.
3. **Pre-sleep winddown (30–60 minutes)**

   * Dim lights, stop work, avoid screens or use filters.
   * Do relaxing, low-stakes activities (reading, light stretching, conversation).
4. **Bedroom setup**

   * Dark, quiet (earplugs/white noise if needed), cool.
5. **If awake in bed**

   * After ~20–30 minutes, get up and:

     * Go to another room.
     * Do something **relaxing and non-productive** (no work, no high-stakes tasks).
     * Return to bed only when sleepy. 
6. **If insomnia persists**

   * Discuss **CBT-I** or other structured approaches with a professional.

---

#### Emotional Health Work Protocol

1. **Inventory emotional patterns**

   * Write down repeated situations where you later think, “Why did I react like that?”
2. **Link to health behaviors**

   * For each pattern, note how it affects sleep, eating, exercise, or relationships.
3. **Seek help**

   * Find a therapist or counselor experienced with **family systems, trauma, and health behavior change**.
4. **Practice new responses**

   * Work on one pattern at a time: e.g., replacing explosive reactions with time-outs and calm communication.
5. **Review regularly**

   * Notice how emotional progress changes the **ease and consistency** of your health habits.

#### Medical Screening Checklist

*(To discuss with your clinician; not a prescription.)*

* □ Family history reviewed for **early or severe heart disease, cancer, dementia, or diabetes**. 
* □ Blood pressure and basic labs checked regularly.
* □ Metabolic status assessed well before frank diabetes (e.g., values trending upward even if “normal”). 
* □ Lipids assessed with **more informative markers** if available, not just total and LDL cholesterol.
* □ Age- and risk-appropriate **cancer screenings** discussed and scheduled.
* □ Sleep issues (snoring, apneas, chronic insomnia) evaluated rather than ignored.
* □ Emotional health concerns acknowledged and referrals considered.

### Frameworks & Models

#### Four Horsemen of Chronic Disease

* **Atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease**

  * Heart attacks, strokes, peripheral vascular disease.
* **Cancer**

  * Various malignancies with long preclinical phases.
* **Neurodegenerative disease**

  * Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias, Parkinsonian disorders.
* **Metabolic disease/type 2 diabetes and related dysfunction**

  * Insulin resistance, fatty liver, obesity and its consequences.

> Most people will die of one or more of these; **longevity work is largely Horseman-management work.** 

---

#### Medicine 2.0 vs Medicine 3.0

* **Medicine 2.0 (status quo)**

  * Focus: **Diagnose and treat disease** once clinically apparent.
  * Tools: Guidelines, binary thresholds (healthy/sick), acute interventions.
  * Timing: Late; often starts when eggs are already falling off the building. 

* **Medicine 3.0 (Attia’s vision)**

  * Focus: **Delay or prevent disease**, extend healthspan.
  * Tools: Early risk detection, continuous metrics, personalized interventions.
  * Timing: Early; aims to act **years or decades before diagnosis**.
  * Mindset: Probabilistic, iterative, more akin to engineering or investing than to emergency response.

#### Objective–Strategy–Tactics

* **Objective:**

  * E.g., “Live to 90+ with enough function to play with grandchildren, travel independently, and think clearly.”
* **Strategy:**

  * High-level approach to achieve the objective:

    * Reduce risk from primary Horseman(s).
    * Build and maintain functional capacity for the Centenarian Decathlon tasks.
    * Support mental and emotional resilience. 
* **Tactics:**

  * Concrete actions you can do this week:

    * Schedule lab work.
    * Add two weekly strength sessions.
    * Remove sugary drinks from the house.
    * Implement a consistent sleep schedule.

> Tactics change often. Strategy changes occasionally. Objective changes rarely.

#### Centenarian Decathlon

* A thought exercise: **List the physical tasks you want to be able to perform in your 80s–90s** (e.g., climb stairs, get off the floor, carry groceries, travel with luggage).
* These become your **training targets**; you then work backward from those demands to what you need to be able to do at every preceding decade. 

Examples of events:

* Walking a certain distance without stopping.
* Carrying 20–30+ pounds (groceries, luggage).
* Getting off the floor without assistance.
* Getting in and out of a car or bathtub safely.
* Climbing multiple flights of stairs.

#### Three Levers of Nutrition

As framed in the book:

1. **Calorie restriction**

   * Total energy intake over time; primary driver of weight change.
2. **Dietary restriction**

   * What you do and don’t eat (macro ratios, food types, special patterns).
3. **Time restriction**

   * When you eat (meal timing, fasting windows). 

> Different combinations of these levers can work; the right combination is the sustainable one that improves your risk markers and function.

### Guardrails & Caveats

* **No book (or handbook) can replace a personal physician**

  * Individual conditions, medications, and risks can make some tactics unsafe for you. Always adapt with medical guidance. 

* **Avoid ideology in diet and training**

  * Beware of anyone claiming a single diet or exercise method is best for everyone.
  * Use **biological response and risk reduction** as your compass, not dogma.

* **Extreme practices have trade-offs**

  * Very restrictive diets, prolonged fasting, or overly aggressive training can backfire, especially when they compromise muscle mass, sleep, or emotional health.

* **Test early, but don’t over-interpret every fluctuation**

  * More data is useful only if it informs better decisions, not obsession or anxiety.

* **Emotional health work can be destabilizing before it’s stabilizing**

  * Digging into deep patterns may temporarily worsen stress or conflict; support and pacing matter.

* **Uncertainty is unavoidable**

  * Most decisions in longevity are about **probabilities, not guarantees**. Aim for better odds and better trajectories, not perfection.

### Key Terms & Concepts

* **Lifespan** – How long you live (years alive).
* **Healthspan** – The period of life when you are functionally well, not just alive. 
* **Four Horsemen** – The four main chronic disease categories that drive slow death: cardiovascular disease, cancer, neurodegeneration, and metabolic disease.
* **Medicine 3.0** – A proactive, personalized, data-informed practice of medicine focused on preventing or delaying chronic disease.
* **Centenarian Decathlon** – A set of physical tasks you want to be able to do in your 80s–90s that guide your current training.
* **Metabolic dysfunction** – A spectrum of abnormalities in glucose, insulin, and related parameters that precedes type 2 diabetes and drives many chronic diseases. 
* **Insulin resistance** – A state where tissues respond poorly to insulin, leading to higher insulin and eventually higher glucose levels.
* **CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)** – A structured, evidence-based therapy for chronic insomnia focused on changing behaviors and thoughts about sleep. 
* **Stability** – The ability to control movement and maintain posture and balance, especially under load or perturbation.
