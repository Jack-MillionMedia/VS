# Cell Membrane and Transport

## Membrane Structure: Fluid Mosaic Model

**Phospholipid Bilayer:**

- **Hydrophilic heads**: Face aqueous environments (intracellular and extracellular)
- **Hydrophobic tails**: Face each other in interior
- **Amphipathic**: Both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions

**Membrane Proteins:**

1. **Integral proteins**: Embedded in membrane; span bilayer (transmembrane proteins)
   - Receptors, channels, transporters
2. **Peripheral proteins**: Attached to membrane surface
   - Enzymes, structural proteins

**Membrane Lipids:**

- **Phospholipids**: Phosphatidylcholine, phosphatidylethanolamine, phosphatidylserine
- **Cholesterol**: Modulates membrane fluidity
- **Glycolipids**: Carbohydrate chains; cell recognition

**Membrane Carbohydrates (Glycocalyx):**

- Attached to proteins (glycoproteins) and lipids (glycolipids)
- Cell recognition, adhesion, immune function

## Transport Across Membranes

### Passive Transport (No ATP Required)

**Simple Diffusion:**

- Movement down concentration gradient
- **Lipophilic molecules** cross freely: O₂, CO₂, steroids, fat-soluble vitamins
- Small uncharged polar molecules: H₂O, ethanol, urea

**Facilitated Diffusion:**

- Down concentration gradient via carriers or channels
- **Glucose transporters (GLUTs)**: GLUT1-14 (tissue-specific)
- **Ion channels**: Na+, K+, Ca²+, Cl- channels
- **Aquaporins**: Water channels

**Osmosis:**

- Water movement across semipermeable membrane
- Toward higher solute concentration

### Active Transport (Requires ATP)

**Primary Active Transport:**

- Direct use of ATP
- **Na+-K+-ATPase** (sodium-potassium pump):
  - 3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in
  - Maintains electrochemical gradient
  - Essential for nerve/muscle function
- **Ca²+-ATPase**: Pumps Ca²+ out of cytoplasm
- **H+-K+-ATPase**: Gastric acid secretion

**Secondary Active Transport:**

- Uses electrochemical gradient created by primary transport
- **Symport**: Both solutes move same direction
  - Na+-glucose symporter (SGLT)
  - Na+-amino acid symporters
- **Antiport**: Solutes move opposite directions
  - Na+-Ca²+ exchanger
  - Na+-H+ exchanger

### Bulk Transport

**Endocytosis:**

- **Phagocytosis**: "Cell eating"; large particles
- **Pinocytosis**: "Cell drinking"; fluids and small solutes
- **Receptor-mediated endocytosis**: Specific molecules bind receptors → clathrin-coated vesicles

**Exocytosis:**

- Vesicles fuse with membrane → release contents
- Neurotransmitter release, hormone secretion

## Herbal Effects on Membrane Function

**Membrane Stabilizers:**

- **Anthocyanins, flavonoids**: Strengthen membranes; reduce permeability
- **Silymarin**: Hepatocyte membrane stabilization

**Membrane Disruptors (Antimicrobial):**

- **Essential oils** (thymol, carvacrol, eugenol): Disrupt bacterial membranes
- **Berberine**: Bacterial membrane damage
- **Saponins**: Form complexes with membrane sterols → pore formation

**Ion Channel Modulators:**

- **Menthol**: TRPM8 channel agonist (cooling sensation)
- **Capsaicin**: TRPV1 channel agonist (heat sensation)
- **Valerian (valerenic acid)**: Modulates GABA_A receptor-chloride channel complex

# Energy Metabolism and ATP Production

## Cellular Respiration Overview

**Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP (36-38 ATP per glucose)**

### 1. Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)

**Glucose → 2 Pyruvate**

- **Net yield**: 2 ATP, 2 NADH
- **Aerobic**: Pyruvate → mitochondria → Krebs cycle
- **Anaerobic**: Pyruvate → lactate (muscle during intense exercise)

### 2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle, Mitochondrial Matrix)

**Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA → Krebs cycle**

- **Per glucose**: 2 acetyl-CoA, 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 2 ATP (or GTP)
- Generates electron carriers for electron transport chain

### 3. Electron Transport Chain and Oxidative Phosphorylation (Inner Mitochondrial Membrane)

**NADH and FADH₂ → electron transport chain → proton gradient → ATP synthase → ATP**

- **Complex I-IV**: Transfer electrons; pump H+ into intermembrane space
- **Complex V (ATP synthase)**: H+ flows back through → drives ATP synthesis
- **Yield**: ~32-34 ATP

**Total ATP per glucose: 36-38 ATP**

## Mitochondrial Function and Dysfunction

**Mitochondrial Health Indicators:**

- **Membrane potential (ΔΨm)**: Maintained by proton gradient
- **ROS production**: Byproduct of electron transport; excessive ROS → damage
- **Mitochondrial biogenesis**: Regulated by PGC-1α

**Mitochondrial Dysfunction:**

- Neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's)
- Metabolic disorders (diabetes, obesity)
- Aging
- Drug-induced toxicity (statins, NSAIDs, certain antibiotics)

## Herbal Support for Mitochondrial Function

**Mitochondrial Biogenesis:**

- **Resveratrol**: Activates SIRT1 → ↑ PGC-1α → mitochondrial biogenesis
- **Quercetin**: SIRT1 activation
- **Green tea (EGCG)**: ↑ Mitochondrial biogenesis

**ATP Production Enhancement:**

- **Coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10)**: Electron carrier in ETC; ubiquinol (reduced form) is active
- **Cordyceps**: ↑ ATP production; improves oxygen utilization
- **Rhodiola rosea**: Enhances mitochondrial function

**Antioxidant Protection:**

- **Curcumin, green tea, resveratrol**: Reduce mitochondrial ROS
- **Alpha-lipoic acid**: Mitochondrial antioxidant; regenerates CoQ10

# Receptor Pharmacology

**Context:** Most herbal constituents exert effects by binding to cellular receptors. Understanding receptor types and signal transduction is essential for comprehending herbal mechanisms.

## Receptor Classification

### 1. G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs)

**Structure:** 7 transmembrane domains

**Mechanism:**

1. Ligand binds extracellular domain → conformational change
2. Activates G-protein (heterotrimeric: α, β, γ subunits)
3. α subunit exchanges GDP for GTP → dissociates
4. α subunit activates or inhibits effector enzymes/channels
5. GTPase activity hydrolyzes GTP → GDP → reassociation

**G-Protein Subtypes:**

_Gs (Stimulatory):_

- Activates **adenylyl cyclase** → ↑ cAMP → activates protein kinase A (PKA)
- Examples: β-adrenergic receptors (epinephrine, norepinephrine)

_Gi (Inhibitory):_

- Inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓ cAMP
- Examples: α₂-adrenergic receptors, μ-opioid receptors

_Gq:_

- Activates **phospholipase C (PLC)** → cleaves PIP₂ → **IP₃ + DAG**
  - **IP₃**: Releases Ca²+ from ER
  - **DAG**: Activates protein kinase C (PKC)
- Examples: α₁-adrenergic receptors, muscarinic M₁/M₃ receptors, histamine H₁ receptors

**Herbal GPCR Interactions:**

- **Cannabinoids (from Cannabis)**: CB1 and CB2 cannabinoid receptors (Gi-coupled)
- **Echinacea alkylamides**: CB2 receptor activation
- **Salvinorin A (Salvia divinorum)**: κ-opioid receptor agonist
- **Bitter compounds**: TAS2R bitter receptors → digestive secretions

### 2. Ligand-Gated Ion Channels (Ionotropic Receptors)

**Mechanism:** Ligand binding → channel opens → ion flux → rapid electrical response

**Examples:**

_Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptor (nAChR):_

- Na+ influx → depolarization
- **Lobeline** (Lobelia inflata): nAChR partial agonist (smoking cessation)

_GABA_A Receptor:_

- Cl- influx → hyperpolarization → inhibition
- **Valerenic acid** (Valerian): Positive allosteric modulator → ↑ GABA effect
- **Benzodiazepines**: Allosteric enhancers (not herbal but relevant)

_Glycine Receptor:_

- Cl- influx → inhibition
- Strychnine (toxic alkaloid): Competitive antagonist

_Glutamate Receptors (NMDA, AMPA, Kainate):_

- Cation influx (Na+, Ca²+) → excitation
- **Berberine**: NMDA receptor modulation

### 3. Enzyme-Linked Receptors

**Structure:** Single transmembrane domain; intracellular catalytic domain

**Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs):**

_Mechanism:_

1. Ligand binding → receptor dimerization
2. Cross-phosphorylation of tyrosine residues (autophosphorylation)
3. Phosphorylated tyrosines recruit signaling proteins
4. Activate downstream pathways (Ras/MAPK, PI3K/Akt)

_Examples:_

- Insulin receptor
- EGF receptor (EGFR)
- VEGF receptor (angiogenesis)

**Herbal Interactions:**

- **EGCG (green tea)**: EGFR inhibition → anti-cancer effects
- **Curcumin**: Inhibits EGFR, HER2
- **Resveratrol**: Modulates insulin receptor signaling

### 4. Nuclear Receptors (Intracellular Receptors)

**Mechanism:**

1. Lipophilic ligand crosses membrane
2. Binds nuclear receptor in cytoplasm or nucleus
3. Receptor-ligand complex translocates to nucleus (if cytoplasmic)
4. Binds DNA response elements
5. Regulates gene transcription (hours to days for effects)

**Examples:**

_Steroid Hormone Receptors:_

- **Estrogen receptors (ERα, ERβ)**
- **Androgen receptor (AR)**
- **Glucocorticoid receptor (GR)**
- **Progesterone receptor (PR)**

_Thyroid Hormone Receptors (TR)_

_Vitamin D Receptor (VDR)_

_Retinoic Acid Receptors (RAR, RXR)_

_Peroxisome Proliferator-Activated Receptors (PPARs):_

- **PPARα**: Fatty acid oxidation (fibrates target)
- **PPARγ**: Adipogenesis, insulin sensitivity (thiazolidinediones target)

**Herbal Nuclear Receptor Interactions:**

_Phytoestrogens:_

- **Isoflavones** (soy, red clover): Bind ERα and ERβ (selective estrogen receptor modulators - SERMs)
- **Lignans** (flaxseed): Weak estrogenic activity
- **8-prenylnaringenin** (hops): Potent phytoestrogen

_Androgen Receptor:_

- **Saw palmetto**: 5α-reductase inhibition → ↓ DHT (not direct AR antagonism)
- **Pygeum africanum**: Anti-androgenic effects

_PPARs:_

- **Berberine**: PPARα and PPARγ activation → lipid metabolism, insulin sensitivity
- **Resveratrol**: PPARα activation

# Signal Transduction Pathways

## Second Messenger Systems

### cAMP Pathway (GPCR → Gs → Adenylyl Cyclase)

**Cascade:**

1. GPCR activation → Gs → adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP
2. cAMP activates **protein kinase A (PKA)**
3. PKA phosphorylates target proteins → cellular response
4. **Phosphodiesterases (PDEs)** degrade cAMP → terminate signal

**Effects:**

- Glycogenolysis (liver, muscle)
- Lipolysis (adipose tissue)
- Cardiac contractility (β₁-adrenergic stimulation)
- Smooth muscle relaxation (β₂-adrenergic stimulation)

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Forskolin** (Coleus forskohlii): Directly activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP
- **Methylxanthines** (caffeine, theophylline): PDE inhibitors → ↑ cAMP
- **Theophylline** (tea): Bronchodilation via cAMP

### IP₃/DAG Pathway (GPCR → Gq → Phospholipase C)

**Cascade:**

1. GPCR activation → Gq → phospholipase C (PLC)
2. PLC cleaves PIP₂ → **IP₃ + DAG**
3. **IP₃** binds receptors on ER → Ca²+ release
4. **DAG** + Ca²+ activate **protein kinase C (PKC)**
5. PKC phosphorylates target proteins

**Effects:**

- Smooth muscle contraction
- Secretion (digestive enzymes, neurotransmitters)
- Cell proliferation

### Calcium as Second Messenger

**Sources:**

- Extracellular influx (voltage-gated Ca²+ channels, ligand-gated channels)
- Intracellular stores (ER, sarcoplasmic reticulum via IP₃ or ryanodine receptors)

**Ca²+-Binding Proteins:**

- **Calmodulin**: Binds Ca²+ → activates Ca²+/calmodulin-dependent protein kinases (CaMKs)
- **Troponin C**: Muscle contraction

**Effects:**

- Muscle contraction
- Neurotransmitter release
- Enzyme activation
- Gene transcription

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Calcium channel blockers**: Amlodipine (not herbal; pharmaceutical reference)
- **Peppermint (menthol)**: Relaxes smooth muscle via Ca²+ channel modulation

## MAPK Cascades (Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinases)

**General Structure:** Three-tier kinase cascade

**Three Main MAPK Pathways:**

### 1. ERK1/2 Pathway (Extracellular Signal-Regulated Kinase)

**Activation:**

- Growth factors, mitogens → RTK → Ras → Raf (MAPKKK) → MEK1/2 (MAPKK) → **ERK1/2** (MAPK)

**Effects:**

- Cell proliferation
- Differentiation
- Survival

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Curcumin**: Inhibits ERK1/2 → anti-proliferative (cancer)
- **Resveratrol**: Modulates ERK signaling (context-dependent)

### 2. JNK Pathway (c-Jun N-terminal Kinase)

**Activation:**

- Stress, inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1), UV radiation

**Effects:**

- Apoptosis
- Inflammatory responses
- Stress adaptation

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **EGCG (green tea)**: Inhibits JNK → anti-inflammatory, anti-apoptotic
- **Curcumin**: JNK inhibition

### 3. p38 MAPK Pathway

**Activation:**

- Stress, inflammatory cytokines, LPS

**Effects:**

- Inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6)
- Apoptosis
- Cell differentiation

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Resveratrol**: p38 MAPK inhibition → anti-inflammatory
- **Ginger (gingerols)**: p38 MAPK inhibition

## PI3K/Akt Pathway

**Activation:**

1. Growth factors → RTK → phosphoinositide 3-kinase (**PI3K**)
2. PI3K converts PIP₂ → **PIP₃**
3. PIP₃ recruits Akt (also called PKB) to membrane
4. PDK1 and mTORC2 phosphorylate and activate **Akt**

**Effects:**

- Cell survival (inhibits apoptosis)
- Glucose uptake (GLUT4 translocation)
- Protein synthesis (mTOR activation)
- Cell growth and proliferation

**Regulation:**

- **PTEN** (phosphatase): Converts PIP₃ back to PIP₂ → negative regulation

**Clinical Relevance:**

- **Overactive in many cancers** (mutations in PI3K, Akt, PTEN, loss of PTEN)
- **Insulin signaling**: Insulin → PI3K/Akt → glucose uptake

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Curcumin**: Inhibits PI3K/Akt → anti-cancer
- **Resveratrol**: Context-dependent modulation
- **Berberine**: Activates Akt in some contexts (insulin sensitization); inhibits in others (anti-cancer)

## JAK-STAT Pathway

**Activation:**

1. Cytokines (interferons, interleukins) bind receptors
2. Receptor dimerization → JAK (Janus kinase) activation
3. JAKs phosphorylate receptor tyrosine residues
4. **STATs** (Signal Transducers and Activators of Transcription) bind phospho-tyrosines
5. JAKs phosphorylate STATs
6. STATs dimerize → translocate to nucleus → gene transcription

**STAT Subtypes:**

- STAT1, STAT2: IFN signaling (antiviral, immune)
- STAT3: Cell proliferation, survival, immune regulation
- STAT4: Th1 differentiation
- STAT5: Prolactin, growth hormone signaling
- STAT6: Th2 differentiation

**Clinical Relevance:**

- **STAT3 overactivation**: Many cancers
- Autoimmune diseases

**Herbal Modulation:**

- **Curcumin**: STAT3 inhibition → anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer
- **Resveratrol**: STAT3 inhibition
- **EGCG**: STAT1 and STAT3 modulation

## Nrf2/ARE Pathway (Antioxidant Response)

**Activation:**

1. **Nrf2** (Nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2) normally sequestered in cytoplasm by **Keap1**
2. Oxidative stress or electrophiles → Keap1 modification
3. Nrf2 released → translocates to nucleus
4. Binds **ARE** (Antioxidant Response Element) in gene promoters
5. ↑ Transcription of antioxidant and Phase II detox enzymes

**Target Genes:**

- **Antioxidant enzymes**: SOD, catalase, glutathione peroxidase, heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1)
- **Phase II enzymes**: GST, UGT, NQO1
- **Glutathione synthesis**: γ-glutamylcysteine synthetase

**Herbal Nrf2 Activators:**

- **Sulforaphane** (cruciferous vegetables): Potent Nrf2 activator
- **Curcumin**: Nrf2 activation
- **Resveratrol**: Nrf2 activation
- **Quercetin**: Nrf2 activation
- **Rosmarinic acid** (rosemary): Nrf2 activation
- **EGCG** (green tea): Nrf2 activation

**Clinical Relevance:**

- Cytoprotection against oxidative stress
- Cancer prevention (Phase II induction)
- Neurodegenerative disease protection
- Cardiovascular protection

# Enzyme Kinetics and Herbal Enzyme Interactions

## Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

**Enzyme-Catalyzed Reaction:**
E + S ⇌ ES → E + P

**Parameters:**

- **Vmax**: Maximum reaction velocity (all enzyme saturated)
- **Km** (Michaelis constant): Substrate concentration at ½ Vmax
  - Low Km = high affinity
  - High Km = low affinity

**Reaction Velocity:**
v = (Vmax [S]) / (Km + [S])

## Enzyme Inhibition

### Competitive Inhibition

**Mechanism:** Inhibitor competes with substrate for active site

**Kinetics:**

- ↑ Km (appears lower affinity)
- Vmax unchanged (high substrate overcomes inhibition)

**Examples:**

- **Aspirin**: Irreversibly inhibits COX (acetylates serine residue)
- **St. John's wort (hypericin)**: Competes with neurotransmitters for reuptake transporters

### Non-Competitive Inhibition

**Mechanism:** Inhibitor binds different site (allosteric); can bind enzyme or ES complex

**Kinetics:**

- ↓ Vmax (reduces enzyme efficiency)
- Km unchanged

**Examples:**

- Some herbal enzyme modulators act non-competitively

### Uncompetitive Inhibition

**Mechanism:** Inhibitor binds only to ES complex

**Kinetics:**

- ↓ Vmax
- ↓ Km (appears higher affinity)

### Irreversible Inhibition

**Mechanism:** Covalent modification of enzyme; permanent inactivation

**Examples:**

- **Aspirin**: Irreversible COX inhibition (acetylation)
- **Organophosphates**: Irreversible acetylcholinesterase inhibition (not herbal)

## Herbal Enzyme Inhibitors

_See Hepatic-Renal-Detox-Mechanisms.md for detailed CYP450 inhibition_

**COX Inhibitors:**

- Willow bark, meadowsweet, wintergreen (salicylates)
- Turmeric, ginger, Boswellia

**5-Lipoxygenase Inhibitors:**

- Boswellia, curcumin

**Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors:**

- **Huperzine A** (Huperzia serrata): Reversible inhibitor → ↑ acetylcholine (Alzheimer's)
- **Galantamine** (Galanthus nivalis): Reversible inhibitor (pharmaceutical from plant)

**Monoamine Oxidase (MAO) Inhibitors:**

- **St. John's wort**: Weak MAO inhibition (not clinically significant at typical doses)

**α-Glucosidase Inhibitors:**

- **Mulberry leaf** (Morus alba): Delays carbohydrate absorption → ↓ postprandial glucose
- **Acarbose** (pharmaceutical): Competitive inhibitor

## Herbal Enzyme Inducers

_See Hepatic-Renal-Detox-Mechanisms.md for Phase I and Phase II induction_

**CYP450 Inducers:**

- **St. John's wort**: CYP3A4, CYP2C9, CYP1A2 induction → ↓ drug levels

**Phase II Enzyme Inducers (via Nrf2):**

- Sulforaphane, curcumin, resveratrol, quercetin
- ↑ GST, UGT, NQO1

# Phytochemistry: Secondary Metabolites

**Context:** Most therapeutic herbal actions derive from secondary metabolites—compounds not essential for basic plant survival but serving ecological roles (defense, attraction, competition).

## Classification of Phytochemicals

### 1. Phenolic Compounds

**Simple Phenols and Phenolic Acids:**

- **Salicylic acid** (willow): Analgesic, anti-inflammatory
- **Gallic acid**: Antioxidant
- **Caffeic acid, ferulic acid, chlorogenic acid**: Antioxidants

**Flavonoids** (Over 6000 compounds)

_Structure:_ C₆-C₃-C₆ (two benzene rings connected by 3-carbon chain)

_Subclasses:_

- **Flavonols**: Quercetin, kaempferol, myricetin
- **Flavones**: Apigenin, luteolin
- **Flavanones**: Naringenin (grapefruit), hesperidin (citrus)
- **Flavan-3-ols (Catechins)**: EGCG (green tea), proanthocyanidins (grape seed)
- **Anthocyanins**: Red, blue, purple pigments (berries, red cabbage)
- **Isoflavones**: Genistein, daidzein (soy, red clover)

_Actions:_

- Antioxidant (radical scavenging, metal chelation)
- Anti-inflammatory (COX, LOX, NF-κB inhibition)
- Cardioprotective
- Neuroprotective
- Estrogenic (isoflavones)

**Tannins**

_Types:_

- **Hydrolyzable**: Gallotannins, ellagitannins (oak, witch hazel)
- **Condensed (Proanthocyanidins)**: Grape seed, pine bark

_Actions:_

- Astringent (precipitate proteins)
- Antioxidant
- Antimicrobial
- Anti-diarrheal

**Coumarins**

_Examples:_

- Simple coumarins: Coumarin (sweet clover, tonka bean)
- Furanocoumarins: Bergapten (bergamot), psoralen (celery, parsnip)

_Actions:_

- Anticoagulant (dicoumarol from spoiled sweet clover)
- Phototoxic (furanocoumarins)
- Anti-inflammatory

**Lignans**

_Examples:_

- Secoisolariciresinol diglucoside (flaxseed) → enterolignans (enterolactone, enterodiol) by gut bacteria

_Actions:_

- Phytoestrogenic
- Antioxidant

### 2. Terpenoids (Isoprenoids)

**Classification by Isoprene Units (C₅H₈):**

**Monoterpenes (C₁₀):**

- **Essential oils**: Menthol (peppermint), limonene (citrus), linalool (lavender)
- **Iridoids**: Harpagoside (devil's claw), valepotriates (valerian)

**Sesquiterpenes (C₁₅):**

- **Sesquiterpene lactones**: Parthenolide (feverfew), artemisinin (Artemisia annua)
- Anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer

**Diterpenes (C₂₀):**

- **Forskolin** (Coleus): Adenylyl cyclase activator
- **Ginkgolides** (Ginkgo): PAF receptor antagonists
- **Cafestol, kahweol** (coffee): ↑ cholesterol

**Triterpenes (C₃₀):**

- **Saponins**: Ginsenosides (ginseng), glycyrrhizin (licorice)
- **Sterols**: β-sitosterol, stigmasterol (many plants)
- **Boswellic acids** (Boswellia): 5-LOX inhibitors

**Tetraterpenes (C₄₀):**

- **Carotenoids**: β-carotene, lycopene, lutein, zeaxanthin
- Antioxidant, pro-vitamin A

**Polyterpenes:**

- **Rubber** (Hevea brasiliensis)

### 3. Alkaloids

**Definition:** Nitrogen-containing organic compounds, usually basic (alkaline), often highly bioactive

**Classification by Structure:**

**Pyrrolidine/Pyrrolizidine Alkaloids:**

- **Nicotine** (Nicotiana): nAChR agonist
- **Pyrrolizidine alkaloids**: Hepatotoxic (comfrey, coltsfoot)

**Tropane Alkaloids:**

- **Atropine, scopolamine** (Atropa belladonna, Datura): Anticholinergic (muscarinic antagonists)

**Piperidine/Pyridine Alkaloids:**

- **Piperine** (black pepper): Bioavailability enhancer (inhibits drug metabolism)
- **Coniine** (poison hemlock): Neurotoxic

**Quinoline Alkaloids:**

- **Quinine** (Cinchona): Antimalarial
- **Camptothecin** (Camptotheca): Topoisomerase inhibitor (cancer chemotherapy)

**Isoquinoline Alkaloids:**

- **Berberine** (Hydrastis, Berberis, Coptis): Antimicrobial, hypoglycemic
- **Morphine, codeine** (Papaver somniferum): Opioid agonists
- **Sanguinarine** (Sanguinaria): Antimicrobial, toxic

**Indole Alkaloids:**

- **Vinblastine, vincristine** (Catharanthus roseus): Mitotic inhibitors (cancer chemotherapy)
- **Yohimbine** (Pausinystalia yohimbe): α₂-adrenergic antagonist
- **Ergot alkaloids** (Claviceps purpurea): Vasoconstrictor, hallucinogenic

**Purine Alkaloids:**

- **Caffeine, theophylline, theobromine**: Adenosine receptor antagonists, PDE inhibitors

_Actions:_

- Highly diverse (CNS stimulation/depression, antimicrobial, anti-cancer, cardiovascular)
- Often toxic at high doses

### 4. Polysaccharides

**Types:**

- **β-Glucans**: Immune-modulating (medicinal mushrooms)
- **Mucilage**: Demulcent (marshmallow, slippery elm)
- **Inulin**: Prebiotic fiber (dandelion, chicory)

_Actions:_

- Immunomodulation (via pattern recognition receptors)
- Prebiotic (feed beneficial gut bacteria)
- Demulcent (soothe mucous membranes)

### 5. Volatile Oils (Essential Oils)

**Composition:** Primarily monoterpenes and sesquiterpenes

_Actions:_

- Antimicrobial
- Carminative
- Expectorant
- Rubefacient (topical irritant → ↑ blood flow)

# Bioavailability and Pharmacokinetics

## ADME: Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion

### Absorption

**Factors Affecting Absorption:**

1. **Lipophilicity**: Lipophilic compounds cross membranes easily
2. **Molecular size**: <500 Da generally well absorbed orally
3. **Ionization state**: Depends on pKa and pH (gastric vs. intestinal)
4. **Transporters**: P-glycoprotein, OATPs
5. **First-pass metabolism**: Liver metabolism before systemic circulation

**Bioavailability Challenges:**

- **Curcumin**: Poor absorption (<1% bioavailability)
  - Enhanced by: Piperine, lipid formulations, nanoparticles
- **Quercetin**: Glucuronidated and sulfated → rapid elimination
- **Silymarin**: Hydrophobic; poor aqueous solubility

### Distribution

**Factors:**

- **Plasma protein binding**: Albumin, α₁-acid glycoprotein
  - Highly bound drugs have lower free (active) fraction
- **Blood-brain barrier (BBB)**: Tight junctions + efflux transporters (P-glycoprotein)
  - Lipophilic, small molecules cross more easily
- **Volume of distribution (Vd)**: Extent of tissue distribution

### Metabolism

_See Hepatic-Renal-Detox-Mechanisms.md for detailed Phase I, II, III_

**Phase I (Functionalization):**

- CYP450: Oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis

**Phase II (Conjugation):**

- Glucuronidation, sulfation, glutathione conjugation, methylation

### Excretion

**Routes:**

- **Renal** (urine): Water-soluble metabolites, small molecules
- **Biliary** (feces): Large conjugates (>300 Da)
- **Pulmonary**: Volatile compounds
- **Sweat, saliva**: Minor routes

**Enterohepatic Circulation:**

- Biliary excretion → intestinal deconjugation by bacteria → reabsorption
- Extends half-life (estrogen, morphine, digitoxin)

# Mechanisms of Herbal Actions

## Antioxidant Mechanisms

**Free Radicals and Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS):**

- **Superoxide (O₂•⁻)**, **Hydroxyl radical (•OH)**, **Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂)**, **Peroxy radical (ROO•)**
- Generated by: Mitochondrial respiration, enzymatic reactions (NADPH oxidase, xanthine oxidase), inflammation, UV radiation
- Damage: Lipid peroxidation, DNA damage, protein oxidation

**Antioxidant Defense Systems:**

_Enzymatic:_

- **Superoxide dismutase (SOD)**: O₂•⁻ → H₂O₂
- **Catalase**: H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂
- **Glutathione peroxidase (GPx)**: H₂O₂ + 2 GSH → GSSG + 2 H₂O

_Non-Enzymatic:_

- **Glutathione (GSH)**: Primary intracellular antioxidant
- **Vitamins C and E**: Water-soluble and lipid-soluble radical scavengers
- **Uric acid, bilirubin**: Endogenous antioxidants

**Herbal Antioxidant Mechanisms:**

1. **Direct Radical Scavenging:**

   - Donate hydrogen or electron to neutralize free radicals
   - **Polyphenols** (flavonoids, phenolic acids): Excellent scavengers
   - **Vitamins C and E** from plants

2. **Metal Chelation:**

   - Transition metals (Fe²+, Cu+) catalyze Fenton reaction → •OH
   - **Flavonoids**: Chelate metals → prevent radical generation

3. **Enzyme Induction (via Nrf2):**

   - ↑ SOD, catalase, GPx, GSH synthesis
   - **Sulforaphane, curcumin, resveratrol**

4. **Regeneration of Endogenous Antioxidants:**
   - **Silymarin**: Regenerates glutathione
   - **Alpha-lipoic acid**: Regenerates vitamins C and E

## Antimicrobial Mechanisms

### Antibacterial

**Mechanisms:**

1. **Cell wall synthesis inhibition**: β-lactams (not herbal; pharmaceutical reference)
2. **Membrane disruption**: Essential oils (thymol, carvacrol), saponins, berberine
3. **Protein synthesis inhibition**: Some alkaloids
4. **DNA/RNA synthesis inhibition**: Berberine, quinolones (not herbal)
5. **Metabolic pathway inhibition**: Sulfonamides (not herbal)

**Herbal Antibacterials:**

- **Berberine**: Multifactorial (membrane damage, enzyme inhibition)
- **Allicin** (garlic): Thiol oxidation → protein inactivation
- **Essential oils** (oregano, thyme, tea tree): Membrane permeabilization

### Antifungal

**Mechanisms:**

- **Membrane ergosterol disruption**: Polyenes (nystatin - not herbal), azoles (not herbal)
- **Cell wall β-glucan synthesis inhibition**: Echinocandins (not herbal)

**Herbal Antifungals:**

- **Tea tree oil**: Membrane disruption
- **Pau d'arco (lapachol)**: Inhibits fungal respiration
- **Garlic, oregano oil**: Membrane effects

### Antiviral

**Mechanisms:**

1. **Viral entry inhibition**: Bind viral proteins or host cell receptors
2. **Replication inhibition**: Inhibit viral enzymes (polymerase, protease)
3. **Immune enhancement**: ↑ Interferon, NK cell activity

**Herbal Antivirals:**

- **Elderberry**: Inhibits viral neuraminidase; immune enhancement
- **Lemon balm**: Inhibits HSV attachment and penetration
- **Andrographis**: Inhibits viral replication; immune modulation
- **Licorice (glycyrrhizin)**: Inhibits viral replication (HSV, influenza, SARS-CoV)

# Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Dose-response relationships**: Higher doses don't always = better; some herbs have biphasic effects (low dose stimulates, high dose inhibits)

2. **Bioavailability matters**: Curcumin with piperine; silymarin with phosphatidylcholine (Siliphos®)

3. **Synergy common**: Whole plant extracts often more effective than isolated constituents (entourage effect)

4. **Receptor specificity rare**: Most herbs act on multiple targets (polypharmacology) → broader effects, fewer side effects than single-target drugs

5. **Time to effect varies by mechanism**:

   - Receptor binding: Minutes to hours
   - Enzyme inhibition: Hours
   - Gene transcription (nuclear receptors): Hours to days
   - Tissue repair/remodeling: Weeks to months

6. **Genetic polymorphisms matter**: CYP450, Phase II enzymes, transporters → inter-individual variability

7. **Drug interactions predictable**: Know CYP450 inducers (St. John's wort) and inhibitors (grapefruit, goldenseal)

8. **Phytochemical content varies**: Standardization important; environmental factors, harvest time, extraction methods affect potency

9. **Nrf2 activation = broad cytoprotection**: Sulforaphane, curcumin, resveratrol activate Phase II and antioxidant defenses

10. **Enterohepatic circulation extends duration**: High-fiber diets and certain herbs can interrupt recycling → ↑ excretion
