## Shilajit (Mumijo) — Combined Evidence & Wellness Monograph

**Part 1: Evidence-Based Summary**
Shilajit (mumijo) is a complex, resinous, phyto‑mineral exudate rich in humic substances (notably fulvic acids), dibenzo‑α‑pyrones, and trace minerals. Proposed mechanisms include antioxidant and anti‑inflammatory effects, enhancement of mitochondrial bioenergetics and cellular ATP, and endocrine modulation; fulvic acid and related fractions can also inhibit tau self‑aggregation in vitro, suggesting procognitive potential. Composition varies by source; standardized extracts quantify fulvic acids and dibenzo‑α‑pyrone chromoproteins via HPLC (e.g., PrimaVie). Quality control and contaminant testing are therefore critical (1.1, 2.1, 3.1).

Human efficacy

Testosterone in aging men: A randomized, double‑blind, placebo‑controlled study of healthy men aged 45–55 years found that purified, standardized shilajit (PrimaVie) 250 mg twice daily for 90 days significantly increased total testosterone (~20% from baseline) and free testosterone, and raised DHEA‑S versus placebo; LH and FSH were maintained (per‑protocol analysis, ~75 completers). The product’s HPLC fingerprint specified fulvic acids ≥50%, dibenzo‑α‑pyrones ≥0.3%, and chromoproteins ≥10% (3.2, 3.3, 3.4).
Male fertility: In men with oligospermia treated for 90 days with processed/purified shilajit 100 mg twice daily, total sperm count increased by ~61%, spermia by ~37.6%, motility by ~12–17%, normal morphology by ~19%, and total testosterone by ~23.5% from baseline; routine biochemistry showed no systemic toxicity signals. Study size was modest and design less rigorous than large RCTs, so confirmation is needed (4.1).
Female sexual function: A triple‑blind, randomized, placebo‑controlled trial in reproductive‑aged women (n=43 analyzed) showed that shilajit 200 mg twice daily for 60 days improved total FSFI score and multiple domains (desire, arousal, lubrication, satisfaction) versus placebo; sexual quality‑of‑life scores rose in both groups without a significant between‑group difference. No severe adverse events were reported (5.1, 5.2).
Exercise fatigue/strength: In recreationally active men, 8 weeks of shilajit (500 mg/day) reduced fatigue‑induced decline in maximal isometric strength and lowered serum hydroxyproline versus placebo, suggesting improved muscle/connective tissue adaptation. The low‑dose (250 mg/day) arm did not differ from placebo (2.2).
Cognition: Evidence is preliminary. Reviews and small reports note fulvic‑acid–rich fractions increase cellular ATP/mitochondrial function and inhibit tau aggregation; limited pilot human data suggest possible procognitive effects, but robust, controlled trials are lacking (3.1, 2.1).
Safety, adverse effects, and interactions

Contaminants: Reviews emphasize variability of composition and risk of heavy‑metal contamination; calls for standardizing fulvic‑acid content and routine heavy‑metal testing of commercial preparations. Clinical trials above reported no serious adverse events over 8–14 weeks, but long‑term safety data are limited. Prefer products with documented third‑party testing and defined HPLC fingerprints (2.1, 4.2).
Adverse effects: In the cited RCTs, withdrawals occurred for protocol reasons or mild symptoms (e.g., nausea in one participant in the women’s trial). No consistent laboratory toxicity signals were observed in short‑term use (5.1, 3.3).
Interactions/precautions: Because shilajit may influence androgenic hormones and vascular responses, caution is reasonable with concurrent hormone therapies, androgen‑sensitive conditions, or vasodilators. Individuals with chronic disease or polypharmacy should consult clinicians; ensure products are free of contaminants.
Dosing and standardization used in trials

100 mg twice daily (oligospermia study; processed/purified shilajit) (4.1).
200 mg twice daily for 60 days (women’s FSFI trial) (5.1, 5.2).
250 mg twice daily for 90 days (PrimaVie; men 45–55 years testosterone RCT) (3.3, 3.4).
500 mg/day for 8 weeks (exercise study; high‑dose arm effective) (2.2). Select standardized extracts specify fulvic acids ≥50% with additional dibenzo‑α‑pyrones and chromoproteins; matching the clinical product’s standardization improves external validity (3.2, 3.3).
Strength of evidence and practical guidance

Signals of benefit exist for testosterone in middle‑aged men, semen parameters in oligospermia, aspects of female sexual function, and attenuation of fatigue‑related strength loss; cognition remains a mechanistic/preclinical signal. Trials are small, often single‑center, and product‑specific; replication with larger, longer, and independently funded RCTs is needed. If used, select standardized, contaminant‑tested products at doses aligned with the above trials, and monitor for tolerability and hormonal effects (3.2, 4.1, 5.1, 2.2, 3.1).

Mechanisms proposed from preclinical work: antioxidant/anti‑inflammatory effects (fulvic acids), improved mitochondrial bioenergetics (↑ cellular ATP), modulation of steroidogenesis (DHEA/T increases), and anti‑protein‑aggregation effects (anti‑tau) — but human mechanistic confirmation is limited (2.1, 3.1, 1.1).
Safety and quality concerns: many reviews and analyses emphasize variable composition between sources and the risk of contamination with heavy metals and toxic elements; authors recommend standardized extracts, fulvic‑acid quantification, and routine heavy‑metal testing on commercial products before use (2.1, 4.2). Clinical trials above reported few serious adverse events but were small and short term.
Bottom-line synthesis (practical takeaways)

Evidence strength: Several small, randomized human trials (different indications) and supportive preclinical studies show positive signals (testosterone increase in older men; improved semen parameters in oligospermia; improved female sexual function; some exercise‑performance benefit). However, trials are generally small, often single‑center, and vary in product, dose, and preparation — so certainty is moderate to low and replication with larger standardized trials is needed (3.2, 4.1, 5.1, 2.2).
Dosing used in trials (for reference): common ranges 100–500 mg/day, often given as 100–250 mg twice daily or 500 mg once daily depending on formulation; rely on the exact product standardization (fulvic acid/DCP content) used in trials (4.1, 3.2, 5.1, 2.2).
Safety advice: prefer products with independent laboratory heavy‑metal testing and clear standardization (e.g., declared fulvic acid content / HPLC fingerprint). Avoid untested raw/impure shilajit due to contamination risk; discuss use with clinicians if on hormone‑modulating therapies or with major comorbidities (2.1, 4.2).

### 1. What Shilajit Is

- **Shilajit (Mumijo)** is a dark, tar-like organic–mineral resin that oozes from rock crevices in high mountain ranges such as the Himalayas, Altai, Pamirs, and Tian Shan.
- It forms over long periods from plant, lichen, and resin material that is decomposed and transformed by microorganisms under intense sunlight, temperature swings, and pressure, creating a concentrated mix of humic substances, minerals, and organic compounds.
- In Ayurveda, Shilajit is classified as a **Rasayana** – a rejuvenative substance meant to support overall vitality, resilience, and healthy aging, rather than targeting a single organ or symptom.
- Physical properties relevant for use and storage:
  - Highly hygroscopic (attracts water) and soft/pliable at room temperature.
  - Melts or flows at relatively low temperatures (around 10°C) and decomposes if heated much above ~140°C.
  - Readily dissolves in water but does not dissolve in alcohol or acetone.
- Traditional varieties:
  - Iron-rich Shilajit (“artscha” type) – most widely used clinically.
  - Bitumen-like forms, bee-associated “honey-wax” types, mineral Shilajit from inorganic deposits, and lichen-derived Mumijo; all are variants of an organic–mineral complex with differing proportions.

---

### 2. Composition and Why It Matters for Wellness

- Shilajit is not a single molecule but a complex mixture that typically includes:
  - Humic and fulvic acids.
  - A broad spectrum of minerals and trace elements (e.g., calcium, magnesium, potassium, zinc, sulfur, strontium, selenium, iron, iodine).
  - Amino acids and small peptides.
  - Polyphenols and other antioxidant plant metabolites.
  - Lipids, resins, waxes, steroids, and essential oils in smaller amounts.
- Fulvic and other humic acids may help:
  - Transport minerals into cells.
  - Bind and neutralize some toxins and heavy metals.
  - Modulate immune function and inflammation.
- Trace minerals in Shilajit that have particular wellness relevance:
  - **Zinc** – essential for immune function, wound healing, hormone balance, and hundreds of enzyme reactions; deficiency is linked with frequent infections and poor skin healing.
  - **Sulfur** – important for connective tissue (cartilage, tendons), detoxification, insulin function, and hair/skin/nail quality.
  - **Strontium** – supports bone remineralization and may help prevent or slow osteoporosis when combined with adequate calcium, magnesium, vitamin D, vitamin K2, and movement.
  - **Selenium** – works with vitamin E in antioxidant enzymes (e.g., glutathione peroxidase), supports thyroid function, and contributes to detoxifying certain heavy metals.
- Because Shilajit supplies these in a complex, organic matrix rather than as isolated salts, it may act as a **broad-spectrum mineral and antioxidant support**, especially when diet or soil quality is poor.

---

### 3. Quality, Purification, and Forms

- Raw Shilajit scraped from rocks contains soil, sand, rock fragments, oxidized material, and sometimes animal droppings; it must be **purified** before use.
- Purification typically involves dissolving the raw material in water, repeatedly filtering it, and evaporating the water to obtain a clean concentrate.
  - Modern Kyrgyz methods use pure spring water and gentle temperatures, avoiding organic solvents to preserve heat-sensitive components.
  - Some traditional Ayurvedic preparations boil Shilajit with herbal decoctions (e.g., Triphala) and then dry and tablet/capsule the mixture, which reduces Shilajit concentration and adds other plant constituents.
- Quality varies widely between regions and manufacturers:
  - Products that simply grind the carrier rock or use minimal purification contain less active Shilajit and more contaminants.
  - High-quality producers test for heavy metals, microbiological contamination, and consistent levels of key components.
- Common preparation forms:
  - **Internal**: tablets, capsules, powders, lozenges, syrups, solutions, suspensions.
  - **External**: creams, salves, gels, ointments, and (in some countries) suppositories.
- Legally, most oral Shilajit products are marketed as **dietary supplements**, not as registered medicines, so potency and claims are less strictly regulated and differ by country.

- Factors that weaken immunity over time:
  - Chronic stress and constant overstimulation.
  - Lack of rest and downtime.
  - Poor diet, excessive alcohol, and stimulant overuse.
  - Lack of movement and fresh air.
  - Aging and chronic illness.
- Signs of a weakened immune system include:
  - Frequent colds and infections.
  - Slow recovery from illnesses.

### 5. Internal Uses and Potential Wellness Effects of Shilajit

> The following uses are drawn from traditional practice and modern studies summarized in the source text. Evidence quality varies; in serious or chronic conditions, Shilajit is described as a **supportive adjunct** to standard medical care, not a replacement.

#### 5.1 Immune support and infections

- Traditional indications include recurrent infections, “low vitality,” chronic fatigue, and recovery after serious illness.
- Modern experiments suggest Shilajit:
  - Stimulates immune cells such as T-lymphocytes and macrophages.
  - Shows antibacterial and antiviral effects in laboratory tests.
  - Stabilizes and strengthens mucous membranes (gut, respiratory, and urogenital), which act as a barrier against pathogens.
- It has been used in Asia as an adjunct to therapy for:
  - Respiratory infections (e.g., frequent colds, bronchitis).
  - Urinary tract infections.
  - Gastrointestinal infections (gastritis, enteritis, some forms of colitis).

#### 5.2 Gastrointestinal health (gastritis, ulcers, colitis)

- Historical physicians like Avicenna and Shirazi prescribed Shilajit for:
  - Chronic stomach and intestinal inflammation (gastritis, enteritis).
  - Peptic ulcers.
  - Hemorrhoids, often in combination with other herbal preparations.
- Proposed mechanisms:
  - Antibacterial action in the gut (notably via benzoic acid and other components).
  - Zinc and other minerals that support mucosal regeneration and microcirculation.
  - Anti-inflammatory and anti-ulcer effects of fulvic and humic acids seen in animal models of gastric and colonic ulcers.
- As a result, Shilajit is presented as a potential adjunct for:
  - Chronic gastritis, reflux-related irritation, ulcer risk situations.
  - Some forms of inflammatory bowel disease (e.g., ulcerative colitis), always alongside specialist care.

#### 5.3 Hemorrhoids and venous circulation

- Traditional use includes internal and external application for hemorrhoids.
- The text attributes potential benefit to:
  - Improved microcirculation and venous tone.
  - Anti-inflammatory and local antimicrobial actions.
- It is suggested as complementary to dietary fiber, hydration, and movement, not as a stand-alone cure.

#### 5.4 Bone healing and osteoporosis

- In experimental fracture models and clinical use, Shilajit has been associated with:
  - Faster bone healing and earlier callus formation compared with controls.
  - Better organization of new bone tissue and more robust blood vessel growth in the healing region.
- Strontium, present in Shilajit, is highlighted as a key contributor:
  - Acts similarly to calcium in bone but preferentially enhances remineralization and may help prevent fractures and osteoporosis.
- Broader bone-health guidance from the text includes:
  - Adequate daily intake of calcium and magnesium, ideally from both diet and supplements when needed.
  - Sufficient vitamin D3 (sunlight exposure and/or supplements).
  - Adequate vitamin K2 for proper calcium deposition in bone rather than in arteries.
  - Regular weight-bearing and muscle-strengthening exercise.
- Shilajit is therefore presented as part of a **bone-supportive package**, especially where diet, hormonal changes (e.g., menopause), or digestive issues impair mineral uptake.

#### 5.5 Mineral metabolism and trace-element deficiency

- Because Shilajit contains many trace elements bound to humic substances, it may help:
  - Support people with marginal mineral intake or absorption issues.
  - Address symptoms related to low zinc, sulfur, selenium, magnesium, or strontium status (e.g., brittle hair/nails, connective tissue weakness, poor wound healing).
- It is not a replacement for targeted high-dose mineral therapy in severe deficiency but may help cover everyday gaps, especially when soil and food quality are poor.

#### 5.6 Bronchial and respiratory conditions

- Shilajit has been used traditionally for chronic bronchitis, asthma, and other bronchial disorders.
- Modern work suggests:
  - Antiviral effects against certain respiratory viruses (e.g., inhibition of herpes viruses and respiratory syncytial virus in vitro, through direct viral inactivation and interference with viral attachment to cells).
  - Immunomodulatory support for mucous membranes of the respiratory tract.
- The text suggests Shilajit may be used preventively in people prone to respiratory infections, while emphasizing that acute and chronic lung diseases require proper medical treatment.

#### 5.7 Erectile dysfunction, fertility, and hormone balance

- Traditional usage includes treatment of “impotence” and male infertility.
- Studies summarized in the text report that Shilajit:
  - Can improve sperm count, motility, and other semen parameters in some men with subfertility.
  - May increase testosterone levels and other androgens within physiological limits, without anabolic-steroid-like side effects.
  - Supports libido and erectile function in cases without severe vascular disease.
- Shilajit is portrayed as particularly useful in stress-related or age-related declines in sexual vitality, as part of a broader lifestyle and medical approach.

#### 5.8 Anti-allergic and antioxidant effects

- Components of Shilajit exhibit:
  - Mast-cell stabilizing and histamine-modulating actions in experimental systems, which may help explain traditional use for allergic conditions such as hay fever.
  - Strong antioxidant capacity, neutralizing several types of free radicals and reducing oxidative stress in tissues.
- Potential wellness applications include:
  - Support in seasonal allergies and mild atopic tendencies.
  - General oxidative-stress reduction, especially in people exposed to environmental pollutants, heavy metal burdens, or chronic inflammation.
- As with all allergy treatment, Shilajit is complementary; severe allergic disease and asthma require specialist care.

#### 5.9 Performance, energy, and chronic fatigue

- Shilajit has a long history of use among high-altitude populations, soldiers, and athletes to enhance stamina and recovery.
- Studies cited in the text report that Shilajit:
  - Increased muscle mass by around 10% in some training programs.
  - Prolonged muscle performance and improved tolerance to low oxygen at high altitude.
  - Shortened recovery times and improved resilience to physical and psychological stress.
  - Activated cellular energy metabolism and improved markers of chronic fatigue in animal experiments.
- A short-term “performance” protocol described is approximately **0.3 g twice daily on an empty stomach for 10–12 days**, sometimes combined with juices and egg yolk; this is presented as an example regimen, not a universal rule.
- The text emphasizes that Shilajit has none of the hormonal side effects of anabolic steroids and is not categorized as such, but athletes should always check current anti-doping regulations.

#### 5.10 Brain health, memory, Alzheimer’s disease, and dementia

- Dementia and Alzheimer’s disease are framed as complex, multifactorial conditions that are currently incurable; existing drugs can only slow progression and alleviate symptoms to some degree.
- Shilajit is not presented as a cure, but several potential roles are outlined:

### 7. Dosage, Safety, and Side Effects (as Summarized in the Text)

> These data are primarily derived from animal experiments and decades of clinical experience, as compiled by the author. They are **not** a personalized dosing recommendation.

- General safety from compiled animal data:
  - Daily doses between **50 mg and 800 mg** showed no harmful effects.
  - A single dose of about **200 mg** is considered a practical “ideal” amount for many uses.
  - Standard schedules use 1 dose **2–3 times per day**, adjusted to the condition and individual tolerance.
  - A dose of **200 mg** stimulated bile secretion in studies.
  - In rats, doses up to **10,000 mg per kg** body weight were not acutely toxic.
  - Treatment courses of up to **60 days** at **0.2–0.4 g per day** were reported without toxic side effects.
- Reported side effects at higher or therapeutic doses:
  - Occasional burning sensation at fracture sites in patients with broken bones.
  - In ulcerative colitis, transient heat, burning, weakness, and sweating 40–60 minutes after dosing.
  - At **1–1.5 g/day**, some people experienced slight temperature elevation (~37.5°C/99.5°F), headaches, or sweating lasting 20 minutes to 2 hours.
  - Brief episodes of flatulence during the first days in some users, usually resolving spontaneously.
- Preventive dosing for children (from the book’s box summary):
  - About **1.5–2 mg per kg body weight per dose**, taken **twice daily** (e.g., a 20 kg child takes 30–40 mg per dose).
  - Half-dose recommended up to age 14, then adult doses thereafter.
  - Bitter taste can be masked by mixing with honey.
- Absolute and relative cautions:
  - Use only purified, quality-controlled products.
  - People with serious chronic diseases, multiple medications, pregnancy, or breastfeeding should use Shilajit only under medical supervision.
  - Shilajit is not a substitute for antibiotics, chemotherapy, or other essential treatments, even when used as a supportive measure.

### 8. Practical Tips for Using Shilajit Safely and Effectively

- **Choose quality**:
  - Look for products that specify their origin, purification method, and testing for heavy metals and contaminants.
  - Avoid unprocessed raw rock or cheap powders of unknown origin.
- **Forms and combinations**:
  - Capsules and tablets are convenient and stable for internal use.
  - External creams or salves can be helpful for skin and wound indications.
  - Some formulas combine Shilajit with vitamin D/K2, omega-3, turmeric, magnesium, zinc, or manuka honey to target bone, immune, or wound-healing support synergistically.
- **How to take**:
  - Because Shilajit dissolves easily in water and breaks down at high temperatures, it is usually taken dissolved in warm (not boiling) water, milk, or herbal tea, or as standardized capsules.
  - For better taste and potential synergy, the text often suggests mixing with honey or certain juices.
- **Monitor your response**:
  - Start with lower doses to check tolerance before moving toward commonly used daily amounts.
  - Watch for changes in digestion, sleep, energy, or skin and discuss them with a clinician.
- **Integrate with lifestyle**:
  - The greatest benefits are expected when Shilajit is combined with:
    - A nutrient-dense diet rich in whole foods.
    - Adequate sleep and stress management.
    - Regular movement and, for bone health, weight-bearing exercise.
    - Avoidance of smoking and excess alcohol.
